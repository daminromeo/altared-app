You get two proposals in your inbox on the same afternoon. One says "full-service planning" and quotes $6,500. The other says "day-of coordination" and quotes $1,200. They both use the word "coordinate." They both promise a smooth wedding day. And to a newly engaged couple, they can look like the same service at wildly different prices, which makes the cheaper one feel like the obvious answer.

It usually isn't that simple. A wedding planner and a day-of coordinator are not the same role, and the difference shows up in your contract, your budget, and how much of your sanity you keep between now and the wedding. One can cost $3,000-$8,000+. The other is typically $800-$1,800. Neither is wrong. They are just different tools for different situations, and the couples who overpay (or under-hire) are usually the ones who never learned where the line sits.

Here are the five differences that actually matter when you are comparing quotes.

## 1. Timeline ownership: who builds the plan, and when

This is the difference everything else hangs off of.

A planner builds your timeline from month one. They start with a blank calendar 12 or more months out and construct the whole thing: when to book the venue, when deposits are due, when to send save-the-dates, when to do your tastings and fittings, how the day itself flows minute to minute. They own that document and update it as things move.

A coordinator inherits it 4-6 weeks out. By the time they step in, the plan is supposed to already exist. Their job is to take what you built, pressure-test it, and run it on the day. If you show up to that 6-week meeting without a timeline, ceremony order, or vendor contact list, a good coordinator will build one fast, but that is triage, not planning.

The practical read: if the phrase "12 months of logistics" makes your stomach drop, you are looking for a planner. If you actually enjoy the spreadsheets and just want someone to run the show so you are not answering the florist's calls during cocktail hour, a coordinator is probably enough.

## 2. Vendor sourcing: who finds and books your team

Planners negotiate and book your entire vendor team. They have relationships, they know who is reliable, and they know what a fair rate looks like in your area. They will pull options that fit your style and budget, sit in on the calls, read the fine print, and manage the back-and-forth so you are choosing between three good caterers instead of researching thirty.

Coordinators manage whoever you already hired. They do not go find your photographer or negotiate your bar package. They take your finalized vendor list, confirm arrival times, chase down certificates of insurance, and make sure everyone lands where they should on the day.

That gap matters most when you have not booked much yet. If you are early and staring down an empty vendor list, a coordinator will happily execute an event you still have to assemble yourself. If your team is already locked, you are paying a planner for sourcing work that is mostly done.

### A quick gut check on where you are

1. Do you have a venue booked? If no, lean planner.
2. Are your "big five" (venue, catering, photo, music, florals) contracted? If no, lean planner.
3. Do you have a written timeline and budget? If no, lean planner (or be ready to build both before a coordinator can help).
4. Are you comfortable reading and negotiating contracts yourself? If no, lean planner.

The more "no" answers, the more a planner earns their fee.

## 3. Budget tracking: who watches the money

Planners track every line, often saving 10-15%. That savings comes from a few places: they know the going rate, so they catch when a quote is high; they know which upgrades are worth it and which are padding; and they keep a running budget so a $400 overage here and a $600 overage there do not quietly become a $4,000 problem you discover the week before the wedding.

Coordinators don't touch the numbers. Budget management is simply outside their scope. They are not tracking your spend, flagging overages, or renegotiating anything. If saving money is a core reason you are hiring help, that is a planner job, not a coordinator one.

Here is the math couples miss. On a $40,000 wedding, a planner saving 10-15% is finding $4,000-$6,000. A planner at the lower end of $3,000-$8,000+ can, in that scenario, come close to paying for themselves in negotiated savings alone. It does not always shake out that neatly, but it reframes the fee from "extra cost" to "cost of the person watching all the other costs." ## 4. The cost gap: why one is roughly 3x the other

Let's put the numbers side by side, because this is where the sticker shock lives.

- **Full planners run $3,000-$8,000+.** You are paying for 12 or more months of labor, vendor relationships, negotiation, and the mental load of the entire event living in someone else's brain instead of yours.
- **Day-of coordinators are typically $800-$1,800.** You are paying for a defined window of work, roughly the last month plus the wedding day itself.

One costs about 3x more, and sometimes that is exactly right. The cost gap is not a markup or a scam, it is a scope difference. A planner is doing months of work a coordinator never signed up for. When you compare the two quotes as if they buy the same thing, the coordinator always "wins" on price and you can end up under-hiring for a wedding that actually needed a planner.

The reverse happens too. Couples who are organized, have most vendors booked, and just need day-of execution sometimes get talked into a full planning package and pay planner money for coordinator needs.

## 5. Contract scope: read what you are actually buying

This is the single most important thing to check before you sign, and the easiest to skim past.

Planner contracts run 12+ months of deliverables. Expect language about vendor sourcing, budget management, timeline creation, design, unlimited (or a large number of) meetings, and day-of execution. It is a long document because the job is long.

Coordinator scope is the day itself. That is it. A clean coordinator contract will name a start point (often that 4-6 week mark), list what they confirm and run, and stop there. Anything before that window is usually out of scope or billed separately.

### Red flags to watch for in either proposal

- **Vague scope language.** "Full support" and "we handle everything" mean nothing without a deliverables list. Make them itemize.
- **A "coordinator" price on a "planner" promise (or the reverse).** If a $1,200 quote claims full-service planning, either the scope is thinner than it sounds or the price will grow. If a $7,000 quote only lists day-of duties, you are overpaying.
- **The word "day-of" with a start date buried in the fine print.** Most "day-of" coordinators actually start 4-6 weeks out, and that is good. Just confirm it in writing so you are not left alone until the literal morning of.
- **Hours caps and overage rates you did not notice.** A day-of package that covers "up to 8 hours" on a wedding that runs 11 hours means surprise charges.
- **No mention of a backup or assistant.** Ask who runs your day if your coordinator gets sick. A one-person operation with no plan B is a risk on the one date that cannot move.

Before you sign anything, put both proposals next to each other line by line and check that the scope matches the price. ## So which one do you actually need?

The short version, one more time. A full planner is with you for 12+ months, sources and negotiates your vendors, watches every dollar, and builds your timeline from scratch. A day-of coordinator steps in 4-6 weeks out, takes the plan you made, and executes it on the day.

If you already know what you want and just need someone to run the day, a coordinator is probably enough. If vendor contracts, budget tracking, and 12 months of logistics sound overwhelming, a planner earns their fee.

Use this checklist when the proposals land:

1. **Match scope to price.** A $800-$1,800 quote should describe day-of work. A $3,000-$8,000+ quote should describe months of deliverables.
2. **Count your booked vendors.** Empty list means you probably need sourcing (planner). Full list means you need execution (coordinator).
3. **Be honest about the budget.** If you want someone finding that 10-15% savings, that is a planner job.
4. **Read the start date.** Confirm in writing when a coordinator actually begins.
5. **Check for hidden hour caps, overage rates, and backup staffing** before you sign.
6. **Compare quotes side by side** rather than judging each in isolation.

Save this for when you are comparing proposals, and send it to the friend who just got engaged and has no idea which one to hire. The goal is not to spend the most or the least. It is to buy the exact scope your wedding needs, and to know which one you are signing before the ink dries.
