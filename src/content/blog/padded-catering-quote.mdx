A couple sits across from a caterer, thrilled. the number on the page reads $85 per person. they run the math for 120 guests in their heads, feel good about it, and start talking cake flavors. six weeks later the contract lands in their inbox and the total is closer to $16,000, not the $10,200 they'd penciled in. nothing shady happened. the caterer never lied. the quote just wasn't the whole story, and nobody asked the three questions that would have surfaced the rest.

your catering quote is rarely what you'll actually pay. the per-head number usually excludes staffing, rentals, and setup. the service charge, almost always 20-22%, is a fee the venue keeps, not a tip that reaches your servers. and if your guest count drops below the minimum, you're often still on the hook for the full contract floor. none of that is hidden on purpose. it's just spread across pages of fine print that most people skim while daydreaming about seating charts.

so before you sign anything, ask three questions. they take five minutes on a vendor call and they can save you thousands.

## question 1: what's actually included per person?

the per-head price is the headline, and headlines leave things out. when a caterer quotes $85 a head, that number might cover the food and nothing else. staffing, rentals, and setup are frequently line items that live somewhere else in the quote, or that don't appear until the full contract.

here's what commonly hides outside the per-person number:

1. **staffing** — servers, bartenders, and kitchen crew, often billed by the hour per person on the team
2. **rentals** — tables, chairs, linens, china, glassware, flatware, and sometimes the cooking equipment itself
3. **setup and breakdown** — the labor to build the room before and clear it after
4. **delivery and travel** — a flat fee or mileage if the caterer isn't on-site
5. **cake cutting and plating** — yes, some caterers charge a per-slice fee to cut a cake you bought elsewhere

any one of these can move the real cost well past the quote. rentals alone can add thousands if the venue is a raw space with nothing but four walls and a parking lot.

so ask it plainly: what does the per-person price cover, and what's billed separately? then ask for those separate items in writing before you decide. a good caterer will answer without flinching. that's the whole point of the question. you're not trying to catch anyone. you're trying to compare two quotes on the same terms, because a $75 all-inclusive quote can easily beat a $65 food-only one once rentals and staffing land.

### why this matters for comparing vendors

if you're weighing two caterers, you cannot compare per-head numbers directly unless you know what each one includes. one might bundle staffing and rentals into the per-person rate. the other might quote food only and add everything else later. the second looks cheaper on paper and often isn't. normalize the quotes first, then compare. more on that kind of side-by-side thinking in our [budgeting guides](/blog/category/budgeting).

## question 2: where does the service charge go?

near the bottom of most catering quotes there's a service charge, almost always 20-22% of the food and beverage total. on a $12,000 order that's $2,400 to $2,640. most couples read that line and assume it's the tip. it usually isn't.

legally, a service charge is typically neither a fee that reaches your servers nor a gratuity in the way you'd expect. it's a charge the venue or caterer keeps to cover their own operating costs, overhead, and administration. in many states and contracts, none of it is required to go to the staff who actually worked your wedding. that means if you assume the 20-22% covered tipping and you also hand out cash or add gratuity lines, you've paid for the same thing twice.

so ask the question directly:

- is the 20-22% a service charge or a gratuity?
- does any portion of it go to the servers, bartenders, and kitchen staff?
- if not, is tipping expected on top, and what's customary here?

get the answer in writing. the language matters. "service charge," "administrative fee," and "gratuity" are legally different things, and a caterer who's straight with you will spell out exactly which one you're paying and whether the staff sees any of it. if the answer is "it stays with us," that's fine and normal, but now you know to budget separately for tips instead of assuming they were baked in.

## question 3: what's the guest-count floor?

almost every catering contract has a minimum. it might be a headcount (100 guests) or a dollar amount (a $12,000 floor), and sometimes both. the minimum protects the caterer, which is reasonable. the problem is what happens when your real guest list comes in under it, and real guest lists shrink constantly. people cancel. plus-ones fall through. that college roommate you haven't seen in years doesn't RSVP.

so here's the scenario worth asking about out loud: what happens at 80 guests if you quoted 100? do you still owe the full $12,000 floor?

in a lot of contracts, the answer is yes. you pay for 100 covers whether 100 people eat or not. that can mean paying roughly $2,000 to $2,400 for meals that never get served. it doesn't make the caterer a villain, but it absolutely should shape the number you commit to.

the fix is to quote conservatively. it feels backwards, because you want to plan for everyone. but the floor is the number you're guaranteeing, so set it at a realistic low end and add guests later if they come. adding heads up to a deadline is almost always easier than clawing money back for a room you overbooked.

before you sign, get clear on:

1. the exact minimum, in guests and in dollars
2. the final headcount deadline (the date after which the number is locked)
3. whether you can add guests after that date, and at what price
4. what happens if you drop below the minimum (do you pay the floor, or a reduced rate?)

## red flags to watch for

most catering quotes are honest. a few are structured to look cheaper than they are, and a handful of vendors get cagey when you start asking real questions. watch for these:

- **a per-head number with no itemized breakdown.** if you can't see what's food, what's staffing, and what's rentals, you can't compare it to anything.
- **vague answers about the service charge.** "oh, that's just standard" is not an answer. where does it go?
- **pressure to sign fast.** a deposit deadline that appears the moment you ask questions is a tactic, not a coincidence.
- **a minimum that isn't written down.** if the floor lives in a conversation and not the contract, it can move.
- **tasting or "custom menu" fees that only surface after the deposit.** ask what happens between signing and the wedding, and what each step costs.
- **gratuity lines stacked on top of a service charge with no explanation.** you might be paying two versions of the same thing.

none of these automatically mean walk away. they mean ask one more question and get the answer in writing. a caterer who wants your business will happily put the details on paper. one who won't is telling you something.

## put it to work before your next call

these three questions work because they force the quote to explain itself. what's actually included per person, where the service charge goes, and what the guest-count floor is. ask them before you sign, not after, and you'll see the real number instead of the headline.

quick recap to save before your next vendor call:

1. **per-head cost:** ask what the per-person price covers. staffing, rentals, and setup often aren't in it.
2. **service charge:** ask if the 20-22% is a fee or a gratuity, and whether any of it reaches your staff. legally it's usually neither.
3. **guest minimum:** ask what happens at 80 guests if you quoted 100. find out if you still owe the full $12,000 floor.
4. **get every answer in writing** before you pay a deposit.
5. **quote your headcount conservatively** so the floor works in your favor.
