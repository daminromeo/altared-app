You got the quote. One number, one line, maybe a paragraph about how excited they are to be part of your day. It looks clean. So you tell your partner "the DJ's booked" and move on to the seating chart.

Then the contract lands in your inbox three weeks later, and it's four pages long. Somewhere on page two there's a line about load-in. On page three, a meal requirement. By the time you actually sign, the number in your head and the number you'll pay on the wedding weekend have quietly drifted a few hundred dollars apart.

Here's the thing worth saying up front: this is not a list of red flags. Most DJs and bands are completely upfront about how they bill. The problem is not dishonest vendors. The problem is knowing what to ask before you sign, because these costs live in the contract, not the quote. Below are the five that come up most often, what they typically run, and the exact questions to ask so nothing surprises you on the final invoice.

## 1. The setup and breakdown fee

The quote covers the hours they're playing. It often does not cover the hours before and after, when they're hauling speakers, running cable, testing mics, and then tearing all of it down at midnight while your guests are eating late-night pizza.

A setup fee typically runs **$150-400 to load in, and the same out**. That's both directions. A full band with lighting rigs and a horn section is on the higher end. A solo DJ with a laptop and two speakers is on the lower end, and some don't charge it at all because they build it into the base rate.

The reason it catches people off guard is that it feels like it should be included. Of course they have to set up. But "playing time" and "on-site time" are two different clocks, and the contract almost always bills them separately.

### What to ask
- Is setup and breakdown included in the quoted rate, or billed separately?
- If separate, is it a flat fee or hourly?
- Does the fee apply to both load-in and load-out?

If the answer is "it's included," get that in writing on the contract itself, not in a text message.

## 2. The overtime rate

The reception is going. Nobody wants to leave. Your maid of honor is begging for one more song, so you lean over to the bandleader and say "can you keep going?" They smile and say yes. That yes has a price tag.

Overtime typically runs **$150-300 per extra hour, and here's the part that stings: it's usually billed in 30-minute blocks**. So if the party runs 20 minutes long, you're not paying for 20 minutes. You're paying for a full half-hour block, sometimes rounded up to a full hour depending on the contract.

Do the math on a realistic night. Say your band's overtime is $250 an hour, billed in 30-minute increments. You go 40 minutes over. That's two blocks, $250 total, decided at 11:40 pm when you are absolutely not thinking about money.

The fix is not to avoid overtime. Sometimes it's the best money you spend all night. The fix is to know the number in advance so it's a decision, not a surprise.

### What to ask
- What's the overtime rate per hour?
- Is it billed in 30-minute blocks, and do those blocks round up?
- Who has authority to approve overtime the night of, and how do we confirm it?

That last one matters. Decide beforehand whether you, your partner, or your planner is the person who can say yes, so a well-meaning cousin doesn't commit you to another $250.

## 3. The travel clause

Most entertainment contracts include travel free within a certain radius, then start charging beyond it. The typical threshold is **30-50 miles** from their home base. Past that, you're looking at **$1-2 per mile, or a flat charge of $200 and up**.

This is the fee that punishes destination-ish weddings, the ones that aren't truly far away but aren't in the vendor's backyard either. A venue 60 miles out doesn't feel like "travel." To the contract, it is.

Run the numbers before you assume it's nothing. A band based in the city, playing a vineyard 75 miles away, at $2 per mile round trip, is charging for roughly 90 billable miles past the free radius. That's real money, and it can be worse if the contract charges per vehicle when the band carpools in two cars.

### What to ask
- Where do you measure travel from, and what's the free radius?
- Is it per mile or a flat fee past that point?
- Is it charged per vehicle or per booking?
- For a late finish, is an overnight hotel stay ever required, and who pays?

If your venue is remote, this one alone can swing your entertainment budget by a few hundred dollars, so it's worth pinning down early. We break down more of these location-driven surprises over in [/blog/category/hidden-costs](/blog/category/hidden-costs).

## 4. The meal rider

Buried in a lot of band contracts is a line requiring a **hot meal for every band member**. Not a granola bar, not the appetizer table. A hot, vendor meal, per person.

For a solo DJ this is a rounding error. For an eight-piece band with a sound tech, it's eight to nine extra covers. Your caterer usually offers a reduced-price vendor meal, but "reduced" still means real dollars, and you have to remember to count those heads when you finalize your catering numbers. Couples routinely forget, then get a catering invoice that's higher than the guest count suggested it should be.

There's also a quality clause worth reading. Some riders specify a hot meal (not a boxed sandwich), and if your caterer's vendor option is a cold box, you may need to negotiate. It's not unreasonable. These people are working a ten-hour day on their feet. But it's a cost, and it belongs in your budget, not in a surprise on the catering side.

### What to ask
- How many meals does the contract require?
- Does it specify hot meals, and does a vendor meal from our caterer satisfy it?
- Does the sound engineer or any assistant count toward the meal total?

Then take that headcount straight to your caterer so it lands in the catering estimate instead of ambushing you later.

## 5. The sound check fee

The last one is the sneakiest because it happens before a single song plays. Some bands, especially larger ones and anyone doing live-streamed or acoustically tricky venues, require **1-2 hours of pre-event access** for a full sound check. And that window can be billed at **$100-250 extra**.

Two things make this add up. First, the fee itself. Second, the venue side of it, because early access to your venue can trigger an additional hourly rental charge from the venue. So a sound check can cost you twice: once to the band, once to the space.

This is genuinely necessary for some setups. A live band in a room with bad acoustics needs the time to sound good. But you want to know it's coming so you can either budget for it or coordinate the timing so it fits inside your existing venue window instead of extending it.

### What to ask
- Do you require a separate sound check window, and how long?
- Is that time billed on top of the performance rate?
- Can it happen during our existing venue access, or does it need early entry?

## Putting the real number together

Take a mid-range band quote and stack the extras a realistic night can add:

1. Setup and breakdown: $150-400 each direction
2. Overtime, one hour: $150-300
3. Travel past the free radius: $200 and up
4. Meals for the group: varies with band size
5. Sound check window: $100-250

None of these are scams. Every one of them is standard somewhere. But added together, they can move your entertainment total by four figures over the headline quote, and that's the gap that ruins budgets, not because the fees are unfair, but because nobody planned for them.

## Watch for these in the fine print

A few phrasings that quietly cost money:

- "Load-in and load-out billed separately" with no dollar figure attached. Ask for the number before signing.
- Overtime that "rounds up to the nearest hour" instead of the half hour. That doubles a short overrun.
- Travel measured "portal to portal" (from their door and back), which can double the mileage you expected.
- A meal clause that includes assistants and techs you didn't know were coming.
- Sound check listed under a vague "production" line with no hours or cost specified.

If a line item has no number next to it, that's your cue to ask for one in writing. A good vendor will give it to you happily.

## Before you sign, do this

- Compare the quote against the contract line by line. They're rarely the same document.
- Get every "it's included" promise written into the contract itself.
- Decide who can approve overtime the night of, and tell them the rate.
- Add the meal count to your catering estimate now, not later.
- Confirm whether the sound check needs extra venue time, and price that too.
