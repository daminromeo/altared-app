You've been to three tours in two weekends. The one you keep thinking about had the light coming through the windows just right, a bar you could picture your friends crowding around, and a coordinator who remembered your names. They slid a contract across the table and asked for a deposit to "hold the date." You skimmed it, felt the room fall in love, and signed.

That's the exact moment most couples miss the numbers that matter.

The venue contract is the most important document you'll sign in the whole process, and it's usually the first big one too. It isn't written to trick you. There's no villain hiding a clause in size-6 font. The problem is quieter than that: the numbers that look small on a tour look very different on an invoice six months later. A guest cap sounds generous when your guest list is still a rough draft. An overtime rate sounds trivial until your toasts run long and the DJ keeps playing.

Here are the six numbers to find before you sign, what each one actually costs you, and the red flags that should make you slow down.

## 1. The guest cap

Start here, because this one can void everything else.

Every venue has a maximum capacity, and it's usually tied to fire code, seating layout, and how much floor the dance floor and bar eat up. The number on the tour ("we can do up to 200") often assumes a specific setup. Ask for the cap that applies to *your* configuration: seated dinner, band, photo booth, lounge area, all of it.

Why it matters more than people think: going over the maximum can void your contract entirely. That's not a fee, it's a cancellation. If your list creeps from 150 to 175 and the room is capped at 160 with your floor plan, you don't get a surcharge, you get a problem. Venues can refuse to host an over-capacity event on liability grounds, and by then your deposit is long gone.

### What to confirm

1. The hard capacity for a seated reception with dancing.
2. Whether that number includes vendors and children.
3. What happens if your final count exceeds it (and get it in writing).

If your guest list is genuinely uncertain, build in a buffer. Book a room rated for more than your working number, not exactly at it.

## 2. The rental window

Most venues give you 8 to 10 hours, and that sounds like a lot until you do the math on setup and breakdown.

Here's the trap: that window almost always includes load-in and load-out. Your florist needs two to three hours before guests arrive. The band needs time to sound check. And at the end of the night, someone has to strike the whole thing, usually before the clock the contract is counting against.

An 8-hour window can quietly become a 4-hour party once you subtract three hours of setup and an hour of teardown. That's fine if you know going in. It's a disaster if you assumed you had until 1am and the coordinator starts flipping lights at 11.

### What to confirm

- The exact start and end times of your access.
- Whether setup and breakdown count against the window.
- When vendors can arrive, and whether early access costs extra.

Map your real timeline against the window before you sign. If the numbers don't fit, you either negotiate more hours now (cheaper) or pay overtime later (not cheap), which brings us to the next number.

## 3. The overtime rate

$250 to $500 per extra hour is standard, and it adds up fast.

This is the number couples underestimate the most, because on a tour it's abstract. Nobody plans to run over. Then the reception is glorious, nobody wants to leave, and the coordinator quietly mentions that the next hour will be $400. Multiply that by a band that also charges overtime, plus a photographer staying late, and a single extra hour can cost more than $1,000 across all your vendors.

The venue's overtime rate is only part of it. But it's the one in this contract, so pin it down.

### What to confirm

- The overtime rate per hour, in dollars.
- Whether it's billed in full-hour increments or prorated.
- Who authorizes the overtime the night of (you, not a well-meaning uncle).

Decide your hard stop in advance and tell your coordinator. A firm end time you chose beats a $500 hour you didn't.

## 4. The damage deposit

The damage deposit ranges from $500 to $2,500, and it's easy to lose if you don't read what forfeits it.

This money is refundable in theory. You get it back after the event if nothing's damaged and every rule was followed. The catch is that "the rules" are often longer and stricter than couples realize. Confetti, open flames, glitter, wine on a historic floor, a vendor who parks in the wrong spot, an exit that runs 20 minutes late: any of these can eat into a $2,500 deposit.

You're most likely to lose it in ways that have nothing to do with actual damage. Cleanup requirements are a common one. Some venues expect the space returned to a specific state, and if your team doesn't do it, they hire someone and bill you from the deposit.

### What to confirm

1. The exact deposit amount.
2. A written list of what forfeits it, in full.
3. When and how the refund is returned (some take 30 to 60 days).

Read the forfeit list out loud to your vendors so everyone knows the rules before the day.

## 5. The food and beverage minimum

Many venues require $10k to $30k in food and bar spend, and this is often the biggest number in the whole contract.

An F&B minimum is a floor on what you'll spend on catering and drinks, separate from the rental fee. If the venue sets a $15,000 minimum and your food and bar total comes to $12,000, you still pay $15,000. The extra $3,000 doesn't buy you more, it just meets the threshold.

This matters because it can quietly double your effective venue cost before you've spent a dollar on décor, flowers, or music. A venue with a "low" rental fee and a high F&B minimum can end up pricier than a place that charges more upfront and lets you cater freely.

### What to confirm

- The F&B minimum in dollars, before tax and service.
- Whether service charge and tax count toward the minimum (they usually don't, which raises your real spend).
- Whether the minimum shifts for off-peak dates or days.

Run the F&B minimum against your realistic per-head catering number. If your headcount can't reach the minimum comfortably, the room is more expensive than the sticker.

## 6. The cancellation cliff

Inside 90 days, most venues keep 50 to 100% of your deposit.

Life happens. Family situations change, jobs relocate, plans shift. The cancellation policy decides what a change costs you, and it almost always gets steeper as the date approaches. The "cliff" is the point where you go from losing a portion to losing most or all of it.

Nobody signs a contract expecting to cancel. But this is exactly why you read it while calm, not while stressed. Know where the cliff is, what triggers it, and whether there's any path to postpone instead of cancel.

### What to confirm

1. The full refund schedule, tier by tier, in days out.
2. Whether postponement is allowed and what it costs.
3. What happens if the venue cancels on you (and what you're owed).

## Red flags to watch for on your walkthrough

None of these six numbers are buried in fine print to deceive you. But some contracts are written more loosely than others. Slow down if you see any of these:

- **Blank spaces or "TBD" on key numbers.** The overtime rate, deposit, and F&B minimum should be filled in before you sign, not "confirmed later."
- **Pressure to sign on the spot** to "lock the date." A real hold gives you a few days to read.
- **Vague setup language.** If the contract doesn't say whether your window includes load-in, that's a number you're guessing at.
- **A cancellation policy that's stricter than the deposit protects.** If you can lose 100% at 90 days but the contract barely addresses venue-side cancellation, that's lopsided.
- **Verbal promises that aren't written down.** "Oh, we always let vendors in early" means nothing if it's not on the page.

If a coordinator won't put an answer in writing, treat that as the answer.

## The quick pre-signing checklist

Before you sign anything, confirm all six in writing:

1. **Guest cap** for your actual floor plan, and what happens if you exceed it.
2. **Rental window** with setup and breakdown counted, start to end.
3. **Overtime rate** per hour and how it's billed.
4. **Damage deposit** amount and the full list of what forfeits it.
5. **F&B minimum** in dollars, and whether tax and service count toward it.
6. **Cancellation cliff**, tier by tier, plus postponement options.

Every one of these is just a number that looks small on a tour and large on an invoice. Knowing them before you sign is the whole game. Save this before your next walkthrough, and send it to your engaged friend who's still comparing spaces.
