You loved your trial. The artist nailed your skin, your liner was sharp for hours, and the number on the quote felt reasonable enough that you signed. Then the final invoice landed three weeks before the wedding and it was $400 higher than the number stuck in your head.

Nothing shady happened. Every one of those extra charges was in the contract. They just weren't in the headline quote, and nobody reads a glam contract line by line when they're excited about how good their lashes looked.

The trial is the beginning, not the total. Here are the six charges that actually hit your invoice, what they typically run, and where to look for them before you book anyone.

## why the headline quote is never the real number

Glam artists quote you the thing that's easy to compare: a per-face rate. Bride gets one number, bridesmaids get another. That's the figure that ends up in your spreadsheet next to three other artists you're weighing.

What that number leaves out is everything tied to logistics and timing. Travel, early starts, products, parking, and what happens if plans change. Those aren't padding or scams. They're real costs the artist has to cover, and they're standard across the industry. The problem is that they're invisible until you read the contract closely or the invoice lands.

If you're still in the comparison stage, this is exactly the stuff to line up side by side. Two artists with the same per-face rate can be hundreds of dollars apart once these six get added in. ## the 6 charges beyond the trial

### 01. travel fee

Most artists charge per mile, usually $1-2/mile plus a minimum. If your getting-ready location is 40 miles from their studio, that's a round-trip charge that can quietly tack on $80 to $160 before anyone touches a brush.

The minimum matters as much as the per-mile rate. Some artists set a floor (say, a $75 travel minimum) even for a short drive, which makes a nearby booking feel expensive and a far one feel like a relative bargain. Ask for both numbers: the rate per mile and the minimum.

### 02. early call

Weddings start early because photos, first looks, and ceremony times pull everything backward. If your timeline has the artist starting before 7 am, expect a $50-150 surcharge, sometimes per artist if you've booked a team.

This one sneaks up on couples with big bridal parties. If you have eight people in hair and makeup and a noon ceremony, someone is picking up a brush at 6 am, and that early-call fee is often non-negotiable because it's tied to when the artist has to wake up, not how long the job takes.

### 03. bridal premium

Here's the one that surprises people most. The bride rate runs 20-40% above the bridesmaids rate. So if bridesmaid makeup is $120, your bride price might be $150 to $170 for what looks like the same service.

Artists justify this with the extra trial time, the added attention on the day, and the touch-ups reserved for you. That's fair. But it means you can't just multiply the party rate by your headcount and add one. Price the bride separately every time you compare quotes.

A quick example of how these first three stack:

1. Bridesmaid makeup: $120
2. Bride at a 30% premium: roughly $156
3. Early call before 7 am: +$100
4. Travel, 40 miles round trip at $1.50/mile: +$60

That's $316 in real cost with only two faces done, and we haven't hit products or parking yet.

### 04. touch-up kit

Want the artist to leave products behind so you can freshen up between the ceremony and the reception? Leaving products behind costs $30-80 and is rarely in the quote. You're essentially buying a small kit: a lip color, a bit of powder, a few blotting sheets, sometimes a mini setting spray.

It's genuinely useful, especially for outdoor summer weddings or long reception nights. Just know it's an add-on, not a courtesy, and it usually isn't line-itemed until you ask or until the invoice shows up.

### 05. parking costs

Hotel or venue parking gets billed back — $20-60 a car. If your artist arrives at a downtown hotel with valet-only parking, or a venue that charges for the lot, that fee lands on your invoice, not theirs.

Multiply it if you've got a team arriving in separate cars. Three artists at a $40 valet is $120 you didn't budget for, and it's the kind of thing that only becomes real on the wedding morning when someone hands over a parking stub.

### 06. cancellation fee

This is the one that costs the most if things go sideways. Drop within 60-90 days and you forfeit 50-100% of the deposit. Wedding timelines change: dates move, party sizes shrink, a bridesmaid drops out and you need one fewer service.

Read the cancellation window carefully. A clause that keeps 100% of the deposit inside 90 days is very different from one that keeps 50% inside 60 days. And "cancellation" sometimes covers reductions too, so trimming your party from eight faces to six close to the date can trigger a partial forfeit even though you didn't cancel the whole booking.

## red flags to watch for

Not every contract is written to trip you up, but some patterns should slow you down before you sign.

- **A quote with no travel or parking language at all.** Silence isn't free. It usually means those charges get decided later, on their terms, and appear on the final invoice.
- **A bride rate that isn't spelled out separately.** If the artist only quotes a "per person" number, ask directly what the bridal premium is. A 20-40% jump should be in writing.
- **A vague early-call policy.** "Early starts may incur a fee" with no dollar figure and no time cutoff means you find out the number after your timeline is locked.
- **A cancellation clause that forfeits 100% with a long window.** Losing the full deposit inside 90 days is aggressive. It's not automatically a dealbreaker, but you should know it's there and price the risk.
- **Products "included" without detail.** Ask whether that means a touch-up kit left behind or just the application. The $30-80 difference hides in that wording.
- **Per-artist fees buried in the fine print.** Some surcharges (early call especially) multiply by the number of artists, not the number of clients. That math changes fast with a big team.

The common thread: if a cost depends on logistics (distance, time, parking, headcount), get the number in writing before you sign. A good artist will happily give it to you. Reluctance to quantify anything is the real warning sign.

## how to compare glam quotes without getting surprised

The fix isn't to avoid these charges. They're standard, and a fair artist earns every one. The fix is to compare full costs, not headline rates, so you're choosing the actual best value.

When you're weighing two or three artists, build each quote out the same way:

1. Start with the bride rate (with the premium included, not the party rate).
2. Add the party services at the bridesmaid rate times your real headcount.
3. Add travel using their per-mile rate and their minimum, whichever is higher.
4. Add early-call surcharges if your timeline starts before 7 am, per artist if that's how they bill.
5. Add a touch-up kit only if you actually want products left behind.
6. Add estimated parking based on your getting-ready location.
7. Read the cancellation clause and note the window and the percentage.

Do that for each artist and the ranking often changes. The one with the lowest per-face rate isn't always the cheapest once travel minimums and early-call fees are in.
