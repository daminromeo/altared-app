Your fiancé's aunt asks if her three college roommates can come. It's one line in a group text. You say yes because saying no feels absurd over a text thread. Three names, thirty seconds, done.

Here's what those three names actually did: they added roughly $255 to $600 in catering, one more $150 to $400 centerpiece if they tipped you onto a new table, three cake slices, three chairs and place settings, and three more printed invitations, menus, and place cards. If they were the guests who pushed you past a venue bracket or past the photographer's second-shooter threshold, they cost a lot more than that.

Adding guests feels free until the quotes come back. That's the whole problem. Every other line in your budget shows up as a number you have to approve. Guest count is the one that hides inside a friendly yes. So before you add one more person, here's exactly what each name touches, and why some costs jump quietly while others jump all at once.

## The two ways guest count hits your budget

There are only two mechanics here, and knowing which is which tells you when a cost creeps and when it leaps.

**Per-unit costs** scale smoothly. One more guest equals one more plate, one more chair, one more invitation. The price goes up in a straight line. You can usually feel these coming because they're predictable.

**Bracket and threshold costs** jump in steps. Nothing happens for a while, and then one guest tips you over a line and the price lurches. Venue tiers work this way. So does the second shooter your photographer will require. These are the ones that blindside people, because the 99th guest costs the same as the 98th, and then the 100th costs an extra several hundred dollars all on its own.

Most of the seven costs below are per-unit. Two of them are threshold costs. Watch for those, because they're where a "just add them" decision does the most damage.

## The 7 costs that scale with your list

### 1. Catering

This is the big one. Catering per-head cost runs $85 to $200, and every added guest multiplies it. There's no rounding, no bulk mercy at the individual level. Ten more guests at $150 a head is $1,500, full stop.

Catering is also the number that makes the whole guest-count question real, because it's usually your single largest line. If you're deciding between an 80-person and a 120-person wedding, that 40-guest gap is somewhere between $3,400 and $8,000 in food alone, before you touch anything else on this list.

### 2. Florals

Florals scale by table, not by person, but guest count drives table count, so it comes out the same. Each extra table means one more centerpiece at $150 to $400 each.

Here's the sneaky part: a single guest can trigger a whole table. If your tables seat ten and you're sitting at 100 guests (ten full tables), guest 101 doesn't just add a plate. They open an eleventh table that needs its own centerpiece. That's up to $400 for one name. It's worth asking your florist what a full table looks like in your setup so you know where those tipping points land.

### 3. Venue tier

Most venues price in brackets, and this is a classic threshold cost. A common structure is that 100 to 125 guests is a new tier. You could sit at 98 guests all the way through planning, feel fine, and then add a small family group that pushes you to 102, and suddenly you're in the next pricing bracket with a higher room fee, a bigger minimum, or a larger space you now have to fill.

Ask your venue for their exact bracket lines in writing. If you're at 96 and the next tier starts at 100, that's real information. It might mean four specific names are worth a harder look than the rest. It's not about cutting people you love. It's about knowing that those particular four cost far more than four plates of food.

### 4. Stationery

Invitations, menus, and place cards are priced per unit, every time. This one feels small per piece, which is exactly why it adds up quietly. You're not buying one invitation per guest, you're buying one per household, but menus and place cards are genuinely per person.

Run the math across every printed thing:

1. Save-the-dates
2. Invitations with response cards and envelopes
3. Menus (often one per seat)
4. Place cards and escort cards
5. Programs, if you're doing them
6. Thank-you cards after the fact

Each item multiplies by your count. A larger guest list doesn't just cost more to feed and seat, it costs more to inform at every stage.

### 5. Wedding cake

Bakers quote by the slice, at $8 to $15 each, every guest counted. This is per-unit and clean. A 100-person wedding is $800 to $1,500 in cake. A 150-person wedding is $1,200 to $2,250. Same cake design, more slices, higher number.

Some couples try to game this by ordering fewer slices than guests, betting not everyone eats dessert. Sometimes that works. Just know that if your baker quotes per slice and you cut the order, you're guessing about your own guests' appetites, and running short on cake is a very visible way to save a little money.

### 6. Rentals

Chairs, linens, and flatware are quoted per seat, and delivered both ways. Every guest needs a chair, a place setting, glassware, and a share of the linens. It's the least glamorous line on your budget and one of the most reliably per-person.

The "delivered both ways" part matters. Rental companies charge to drop off and pick up, and some structure delivery around your quantity. More guests can mean more units, more trucks, or a higher delivery tier. When you add a table's worth of guests, you're adding a table's worth of everything that sits on and around it.

### 7. Second shooter

This is your other threshold cost, and it's the one people never see coming. Over about 80 guests, most photographers require a second shooter, which adds $600 to $1,200. And that's before you've even talked overtime.

The logic is simple: one photographer can't cover a large room, a getting-ready suite, and a ceremony from two angles. Past a certain size, coverage genuinely needs two people. But from your side it looks like a step: at 79 guests you have one photographer, at 85 you're being quoted for two. Ask your photographer where their line sits and whether it's a hard requirement or a recommendation.

## Watch for these red flags in your quotes

A few things to flag before you sign anything:

- **"Estimated" headcounts that don't match your real list.** If a caterer quotes 100 and you're planning for 130, that low number will feel great now and hurt later. Quote your honest count.
- **Vague venue pricing with no bracket lines.** If a venue won't tell you exactly where the tiers start and stop, you can't plan around them. Get the thresholds in writing.
- **Photographer packages that hide the second-shooter cost.** Some list it as an add-on you'll "probably need." Ask directly whether it's required at your guest count and what it costs.
- **Per-unit quotes with no total.** Stationery and rentals often come as per-piece pricing. Always multiply it out against your actual count so the number is real, not theoretical.
- **Delivery and pickup fees buried at the bottom of a rental quote.** They're easy to skim past and they move with your order size.

## What to do before you add one more name

None of this means cut the list. It means know what each name actually costs before you send the save-the-date. Here's the short version:

1. **Get your caterer's exact per-head rate** and multiply it by your real count, not a hopeful one.
2. **Ask your venue for its bracket lines in writing** so you know which guest tips you into a new tier.
3. **Ask your florist how many seats fill a table** so you know when the next centerpiece kicks in.
4. **Confirm your photographer's second-shooter threshold** and whether it's required or optional.
5. **Multiply out every per-unit line** (stationery, cake, rentals) against your count before you approve it.
6. **Know your two threshold numbers** (venue bracket and second-shooter line) and treat the guests near them as the expensive ones they are.
