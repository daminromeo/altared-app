You get the PDF back three days after your inquiry call. It's clean. Twelve farm tables at a per-item rate, 96 chairs, a dance floor, some string lights, a subtotal at the bottom that fits your budget with room to spare. You screenshot it, send it to your partner, and feel like the reception is basically handled.

Then the final invoice lands the week after your wedding, and it's several hundred dollars higher than the number you screenshotted. Nothing was added. The tables, chairs, floor, and lights are all there. What's different is everything the quote didn't show you: delivery billed twice, a damage waiver skimmed off the top, setup labor by the hour, a fuel line you'd never heard of, and a late-return charge because your coordinator got the pickup window wrong.

Here's the thing to internalize before contract week: the rental quote is not the rental total. The pretty PDF they send at inquiry is the aspirational number. The real number lives in the contract, or worse, on the invoice after the day is already over and you can't negotiate anything. Below are the five line items that most reliably turn a clean quote into a bigger bill, with what to ask before you sign.

## the quote is a menu, not a bill

Most rental companies build their inquiry quote around the items themselves, because that's the part you asked about. You wanted twelve tables, so they priced twelve tables. What they don't foreground are the service charges attached to getting those tables to your venue, protecting them while you have them, and getting them back.

That's not always shady. A lot of it is standard industry practice, and some of it is genuinely unavoidable. But "standard" and "disclosed up front" are two different things. The five costs below are almost never itemized in the first PDF. They're in the contract fine print if you're lucky, and on the final invoice if you're not.

Read the contract for these specifically. If they're not written down anywhere, that's its own answer, and not a good one.

## 1. delivery, billed both ways

The number one surprise is also the simplest. Delivery gets billed both ways: drop-off and pick-up. People see a single "delivery" line and assume it covers the round trip. Often it's quoted as one direction, or as a flat rate that quietly doubles because the truck has to come back for everything after the event.

If your venue is far from the rental warehouse, or in a spot with limited truck access, this compounds. Some companies also charge more for tight delivery windows, for stairs, for elevators, or for anything that isn't a ground-floor roll-in.

**Ask before you sign:**
1. Is the delivery fee for drop-off only, or does it include pick-up?
2. What's the delivery window, and is there a surcharge for a specific arrival time?
3. Are there added fees for stairs, elevators, long carries, or difficult access at my venue?

Get the round-trip number in writing. If they quote you "$150 delivery" verbally, confirm whether that's $150 total or $150 each way before it becomes a $300 line on the invoice.

## 2. the damage waiver you can't refuse

This is the one that scales with your order, so it hurts the most on big rentals. A damage waiver adds 10-15% of the order total, non-refundable, on top of everything else. On a $4,000 rental order, a 12% waiver is $480 you didn't see in the quote.

A damage waiver is not insurance, and this trips people up. It typically covers normal wear or accidental minor damage, but it does not refund you if you decline it, and it usually doesn't cover loss, theft, or major damage. So you're paying a mandatory percentage for limited protection, and if a table walks off with a guest, you may still owe replacement cost.

### can you decline it?

Sometimes. Some companies let you waive the waiver if you provide your own event insurance or a certificate of liability. If you already have a wedding insurance policy, ask whether it satisfies their requirement and whether declining the waiver saves you that 10-15%. On a large order, that's real money. Just confirm in writing what you're on the hook for if something breaks, because "I'll cover it myself" can mean full replacement value, which is often higher than retail.

## 3. setup labor by the hour

If your rentals just get dropped in a stack by the loading dock, there may be no setup charge at all. But if your venue requires a white-glove install, or you want the chairs placed and the dance floor assembled and the lights hung, that's labor, and setup labor runs $75-200 an hour.

Two things drive this cost up. First, complexity: a dance floor, draping, and a lighting rig take a crew longer than 96 chairs. Second, your venue's rules. Some venues won't let the rental crew leave items unassembled, or require everything installed and struck within a narrow time block, which can mean overtime labor or a bigger crew.

**Watch for the difference between "delivery" and "installation."** These are separate on most contracts. Delivery gets the items to the building. Installation puts them where they go. A quote can include delivery and completely exclude setup, so you assume the tables arrive standing when really they arrive flat-packed by the door.

If your venue requires professional install (many historic and high-end venues do), find that out early, because it changes your rental math meaningfully. Our [planning](/blog/category/planning) posts cover venue-driven costs like this in more depth.

## 4. the fuel surcharge that shows up after

This one is small but infuriating because of when it appears. A fuel surcharge is a flat $50-150 tacked on after the quote, often as a percentage or flat line item that isn't calculated until they route the truck. It's the definition of a cost that lives on the invoice instead of the quote.

Fuel surcharges are common in delivery-based businesses and rise with gas prices. The frustrating part is that it's frequently omitted from the inquiry PDF entirely, then reappears on the final bill as though it were always understood.

**Ask before you sign:** "Is there a fuel surcharge, and if so, is it a flat fee or a percentage? What's the current amount?" If they can give you a number now, get it in writing. If they say "it depends on gas prices at the time," ask for a cap or a not-to-exceed figure so a $50 line doesn't quietly become $150.

## 5. one late day at the full daily rate

Read the return window like it's a flight you can't miss, because the penalty is steep. One late return day is charged at the full daily rate. Not a prorated late fee. The full rental cost of every item, again, as if you rented the whole order a second time.

Here's how this actually happens, and it's rarely the couple's fault. The pickup is scheduled for the morning after the wedding. Your venue's teardown ran late, or the items got moved to a storage room the crew couldn't access on a Sunday, or nobody confirmed the pickup window because you were, understandably, on your honeymoon. Miss the window and the meter runs at 100%.

**How to protect yourself:**
1. Confirm the exact pickup date, time, and location in writing before the event.
2. Assign one person (planner, coordinator, or a specific family member) to be responsible for the return, not "someone."
3. Ask what happens if the venue controls the timeline. If your venue requires everything out by a certain hour but the rental company can't pick up until Monday, who eats that gap?
4. Check whether weekend or holiday pickups cost extra or aren't offered, which forces a Monday return you didn't plan for.

## red flags to watch for in a rental contract

When you're reading the contract (not the quote, the contract), these should make you slow down and ask questions:

- A single "delivery" line with no clarification on round trip versus one way.
- A damage waiver listed as mandatory with no option to substitute your own insurance.
- Any language like "additional labor billed at time of service" with no hourly rate attached.
- Fuel or "logistics" surcharges described as "to be determined" or "market rate."
- A vague return window ("the following day") instead of a specific date and time.
- A quote that's dramatically lower than competitors. It usually means the service costs are unbundled and coming later.

None of these automatically mean the company is bad. Good vendors have all of these costs too. The difference is whether they tell you up front or let you find out on the invoice. A vendor who explains these clearly when you ask is a vendor worth booking. For more on reading between the lines, our [contracts](/blog/category/contracts) posts break down the fine print.

## the real number, before you sign

Knowing these five line items before you sign means you can ask, negotiate, or at least budget for the real number instead of the aspirational one. Here's your contract-week checklist:

1. **Delivery** — confirm it's round trip (drop-off and pick-up), plus any access fees.
2. **Damage waiver** — expect 10-15% of the order total, non-refundable. Ask if your own insurance can replace it.
3. **Setup labor** — $75-200 an hour if your venue needs white-glove install. Confirm whether delivery includes installation.
4. **Fuel surcharge** — a flat $50-150 that often appears after the quote. Get the number or a cap in writing.
5. **Late returns** — one extra day at the full daily rate. Confirm the exact pickup date, time, and who owns the return.
