You get the proposal. It reads clean. A catering total, a bar package, a flat rate for the photographer, a delivery line for the florist. You sign, you plan, you stop looking at it for four months. Then the final invoice lands and the numbers are bigger, but nothing lines up with what you approved. The "service charge" you remembered is now an "admin fee." The photographer's "overtime" is listed as "extended hours." You can't tell if you're being overcharged or just re-reading the same money under a new name.

That gap is the whole problem. Most of these fees are completely standard. They aren't scams. The issue is that the label shifts somewhere between the quote and the final bill, so when you try to audit line by line, nothing matches. You end up either paying without checking or wasting an evening cross-referencing two documents that use different vocabulary for identical charges.

Here are the six fees that do this most often, plus every alias each one hides under. Save this before you sign anything.

## why the names change in the first place

Vendors aren't usually renaming fees to trick you (though a few are). More often, the proposal software, the accounting system, and the final invoice were built by different people, and each has its own default terminology. A venue's booking platform calls it a "service charge." Their bookkeeping exports it as an "event fee." The banquet manager who writes your final bill calls it an "admin fee" out of habit. Same 20 percent, three labels.

The result is the same either way: you can't reconcile what you're paying against what you were quoted. Knowing the aliases is half the work. Here's the full list.

## 1. service charge (a.k.a. admin fee, event fee)

This is the big one and the one people misread most. A **service charge** runs **18 to 25 percent** of your food and beverage total, and it is not the tip. Read that twice, because a lot of couples assume the service charge covers gratuity and then either double-tip or feel bad about not tipping and hand out cash on top.

On a $12,000 catering bill, a 20 percent service charge is $2,400. Whether any of that reaches the staff depends entirely on the venue's policy, and it's often listed separately from a gratuity line. Ask directly: "Is the service charge distributed to staff, and is gratuity included or expected on top?" Get the answer in writing.

Watch for it under: admin fee, event fee, administrative fee, house fee.

## 2. overtime rate (a.k.a. extended hours, overage, additional time)

Your reception runs long. The band plays one more set. Suddenly you owe an **overtime rate** that lands somewhere between **$150 and $400 per hour**, and often several vendors charge it at once. If the party goes 45 minutes over, the photographer, the DJ, the venue, and the coordinator may each bill their own overtime.

That's how a single "we stayed a little late" turns into four separate line items. And it rarely shows up as "overtime" on the final bill. It gets softened into "extended hours" or buried as an "overage."

The math adds up fast. One extra hour across three vendors at $250 each is $750 you didn't budget for, decided in the moment on the dance floor.

Watch for it under: extended hours, overage, additional time, extra hour rate.

## 3. travel fee (a.k.a. mileage, transport, fuel surcharge)

If your vendor is driving to you, expect a **travel fee** of **$50 to $300 depending on the vendor**. Photographers, hair and makeup teams, and florists charge this most. It's fair when the venue is genuinely far. It's less fair when it appears on the invoice of a vendor who never told you their studio was 40 minutes away.

The trap here is that travel is easy to quote as "included" verbally and then itemize on the final bill. Ask whether travel is baked into the package or added separately, and get the mileage radius in writing.

Watch for it under: mileage, transport, fuel surcharge, delivery, out-of-area fee.

## 4. setup charge (a.k.a. installation, strike, breakdown)

Here's the sneaky one. A **setup charge** covers getting your rentals, florals, and decor into place. But the same work at the end of the night, the teardown, gets billed as "strike" or "breakdown." And sometimes you're **billed twice**, once to put it up and once to take it down, without the proposal ever making that clear.

If your florist quotes a "setup fee" of $400, ask flat out: does that include strike at the end of the night, or is breakdown a separate charge? For a full-decor wedding, setup and strike billed separately can each run into the hundreds.

Watch for it under: installation, strike, breakdown, teardown, load-in / load-out.

## 5. cake cutting (a.k.a. dessert service, plating fee)

You bought the cake from an outside baker. The venue still charges you to cut and serve it, at **$2 to $5 per slice**. This is the **cake cutting** fee, and it's the one that most reliably makes couples angry, because it feels like paying for the privilege of eating dessert you already paid for.

For 150 guests at $4 a slice, that's $600 to serve a cake that cost you maybe $500 to begin with. It's real, it's common, and it's almost never in the headline quote. It shows up later as "dessert service" or "plating fee."

Watch for it under: dessert service, plating fee, cakeage, service per slice.

## 6. corkage fee (a.k.a. bar access, pour fee, BYOB charge)

Bringing your own wine or liquor to save money? The venue charges a **corkage fee** of **$10 to $25 per bottle** to open and pour it. On 30 bottles at $20, that's $600, which can erase most of what you saved buying your own.

Corkage is where the aliases get most creative, because venues want to charge it without using the word "corkage," which couples now recognize. So it becomes "bar access," "pour fee," or a "BYOB charge."

Watch for it under: bar access, pour fee, BYOB charge, uncorking fee.

## red flags to watch for on the final invoice

Once your quotes come in, run them against the final bill with these in mind:

1. **A fee that changed names.** If the proposal said "service charge" and the invoice says "admin fee," confirm it's the same charge and not an addition.
2. **Setup billed without a matching strike line, or two charges for the same install.** Ask which one covers teardown.
3. **A percentage-based fee applied to a bigger base than you agreed to.** A 20 percent service charge on food is normal. On food plus rentals plus labor is a bigger number.
4. **Travel or overtime that never came up in conversation.** If it wasn't discussed, question it before you pay.
5. **"Gratuity included" language sitting next to a separate tip line.** You may be tipping twice.
6. **Round numbers that don't tie to anything.** A clean $500 "event fee" with no basis is worth a question.

None of these means your vendor is dishonest. It means the paperwork wasn't written to be reconciled, and that's your job to do before you sign. For more on reading the fine print, our [contracts guide](/blog/category/contracts) walks through what to lock down in writing.

## how to protect yourself before you sign

The fix isn't distrust. It's asking specific questions early and getting answers on paper. A few habits that save real money:

- Ask every vendor to define their service charge and confirm whether gratuity is separate.
- Get the overtime rate in writing and set a hard end time you actually intend to keep.
- Confirm whether travel, setup, and strike are included or itemized.
- If you're bringing your own alcohol or cake, ask about corkage and cutting fees before you assume you're saving.
