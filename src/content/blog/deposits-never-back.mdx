You put down a $4,000 venue deposit in March, feeling like a real adult who is officially planning a wedding. In August, your date shifts because of a family conflict you couldn't have predicted. You call the venue expecting a partial refund, maybe a credit. Instead you learn what the contract already told you in one line on page three: that deposit was non-refundable from the day you signed. It is gone.

This is not a horror story. It is the standard. The venue, the photographer, the florist, the caterer, the band. Every single one takes a deposit before your wedding day exists on paper, and in almost every contract, that money does not come back. It's not fine print buried to trick you. It's industry standard. But knowing exactly what you're locking in, and how much, before you sign is the difference between a budget that holds and one that quietly unravels.

Below are the five deposits that cover the majority of non-refundable cash most couples commit in the first 60 days of planning. Save this for when you're comparing quotes.

## why "deposit" almost always means "non-refundable"

A deposit does two jobs for a vendor. It holds your date, which means they turn away other couples who want it. And it covers work or product they commit to before your wedding happens. A florist who takes your deposit is ordering stems. A caterer is reserving staff. A photographer is blocking a Saturday they could have sold three times over.

Because the vendor takes on real risk the moment they say yes to you, the deposit is structured to be theirs to keep. That's fair. The problem is not that deposits are non-refundable. The problem is that most couples don't add up what they're committing until the money is already spent.

Here's the pattern to watch for in the contract language:

1. **"Non-refundable"** stated plainly next to the deposit amount. This is the honest version.
2. **"Retainer"** instead of "deposit." A retainer is almost always non-refundable by definition, even when the word "refund" never appears.
3. **Silence.** If the contract says a deposit is "due at signing" but says nothing about refunds, assume it's gone. Get the refund terms in writing before you sign, not after.
4. **Rescheduling vs. cancelling.** Some vendors will move your deposit to a new date but never return it as cash. Those are different things. Know which one you're getting.

## the five deposits, and what they actually cost

### 01. venue deposit — typically 25 to 50% upfront

The venue is usually the biggest number and the first one you commit. Deposits typically run 25 to 50% of the total venue cost, and they're non-refundable day one. On a venue quoting $16,000, a 25% deposit is $4,000 you cannot get back the moment you sign.

Venues justify this because your date is inventory. Once they hold a Saturday in peak season for you, that's a booking they can't sell to anyone else. Read the reschedule clause closely here. A good venue will let you move your deposit to another available date if life happens. A rigid one won't, and that's the single most expensive term in the whole contract.

### 02. photographer retainer — $500 to $2,000 to hold your date

Photographers rarely call it a deposit. They call it a retainer, and it typically runs $500 to $2,000 to hold your date. It's gone if you cancel, no exceptions in most contracts.

The logic is simple. A wedding photographer can only shoot one wedding per day. When they hold your Saturday, they've turned down every other couple who wanted it. The retainer compensates them for that lost opportunity, and because a good photographer books 12 to 18 months out, that lost opportunity is real money.

### 03. florist deposit — 30 to 50% due at signing

Florist deposits run 30 to 50% due at signing because flowers are ordered early. This one surprises couples because the flowers don't exist yet, so it feels like you're prepaying for nothing. You're not. Your florist is placing wholesale orders, reserving specific blooms for your season, and blocking labor for your setup window.

If your dream palette leans on peonies or garden roses that have to be sourced early, that deposit is already committed to a supplier before your rehearsal dinner. Cancel, and the florist is often still on the hook for product they can't return either.

### 04. catering deposit — often $1,000 to $3,000

Catering deposits often run $1,000 to $3,000, and they're locked to your headcount estimate. That last part matters more than the dollar figure. Your deposit is calculated against the number of guests you think you'll have, and it reserves staff, rentals, and food orders around that estimate.

Here's the trap: catering costs almost always go up from your first quote, rarely down. If you deposit against 120 guests and land at 145, you owe more. If you land at 95, you usually don't get money back below your guaranteed minimum. Treat the deposit as a floor, not a ceiling, and build your [budget](/blog/category/budgeting) around the higher number.

### 05. band deposit — 50% down, no refund clause standard

Live music holds cost 50% down, and a no-refund clause is standard. Bands and full DJs book prime dates fast, and like your photographer, they can only play one event per night. Fifty percent is a big commitment on a $6,000 band, which is $3,000 locked in the moment you sign.

If you're deciding between a band and a DJ, know that both take deposits, but the band's is usually the steeper percentage. That's worth factoring in before you fall in love with a specific ten-piece.

## the number nobody adds up

Look at what happens if you book all five in your first two months, using the middle of each range:

- Venue deposit (25% of $16,000): **$4,000**
- Photographer retainer: **$1,250**
- Florist deposit (40% of a $4,000 order): **$1,600**
- Catering deposit: **$2,000**
- Band deposit (50% of $6,000): **$3,000**

That's roughly **$11,850 in non-refundable cash** committed inside the first 60 days of planning, often before you've sent a single invitation. None of it is a scam. All of it is standard. But if you hand it over without reading the reschedule and cancellation terms on each one, you've built your entire budget on money you can never recover.

## red flags to watch for before you sign

Most vendors are honest. The deposit structure itself is not the problem. But specific clauses turn a normal deposit into a genuinely bad deal. Watch for these:

- **No reschedule option at all.** Life changes. A contract that won't move your deposit to a new date under any circumstance is the riskiest term you'll sign.
- **A deposit that's "non-refundable and non-transferable."** That word "non-transferable" means you can't even apply it to a different date or service. Push back on it.
- **Escalating balances tied to vague estimates.** If a caterer's deposit locks a headcount but the per-plate price "may adjust," ask exactly when and by how much.
- **Verbal promises that aren't in the document.** If the coordinator says "oh, we'll work with you if something comes up," ask them to write it into the contract. If they won't, assume it isn't true.
- **A deposit due before you've seen the full contract.** Never send money to hold a date on a screenshot or an email. Get the whole agreement first.

If a vendor gets defensive when you ask plain questions about their deposit terms, that tells you how they'll handle a problem later.

## how to protect your budget before you commit

You can't make deposits refundable. That's not the goal. The goal is knowing exactly what you're committing so nothing surprises you three months in. A few habits make the difference:

1. **Read the deposit and cancellation clause first, before the pretty stuff.** Skip to the money terms on your first read of any contract.
2. **Total your non-refundable commitments in one place.** Before you book vendor number two, know what vendor number one already locked in.
3. **Ask every vendor the same three questions:** Is this refundable? Can it move to a new date? What happens if my headcount changes?
4. **Get every answer in writing.** A friendly verbal yes is worth nothing when a deposit is on the line.
5. **Compare full contracts, not just quoted prices.** The cheaper vendor with a rigid no-transfer clause can cost you more than the pricier one who lets you reschedule.
