picture the morning of. you're steaming a dress, your phone is buzzing, and someone's aunt hands you a stack of blank envelopes and asks, "so who are these for and how much goes in each?" you have no idea. nobody told you. the caterer's quote didn't mention it, the photographer's contract didn't mention it, and now you're doing math on the back of a place card while the makeup artist waits.

tipping at weddings isn't optional. it's just not written anywhere obvious. it lives in the unwritten rules that every vendor knows and almost no couple finds out until the week of. so before you stuff the tip envelopes, here's exactly who you're expected to tip and how much, so you can build this into your budget now instead of scrambling later.

## why tips never show up in the quote

here's the frustrating part: a tip is a real, recurring wedding cost, but it almost never appears on the paperwork you're handed. a catering contract will spell out the per-plate price, the bar minimum, the rental fees, and sometimes a "service charge." that service charge is where couples get tripped up, because it sounds like gratuity but often isn't. it can be a house fee that never reaches the people carrying trays.

so you end up with a budget that looks complete on paper and is quietly short by several hundred dollars. across four vendors, tips can add up to more than most couples expect, and none of it is in the number you've been staring at for months.

the fix is boring but effective: treat tips as a line item, not an afterthought. read every contract for gratuity language before the week of the wedding, and set aside cash (or venmo, if the vendor prefers it) in labeled envelopes ahead of time. if you want a shortcut, you can drop a quote into [altared.app](/get-started) and it reads the fine print for you, flagging what's already included so you're not double-tipping a service charge you already paid.

now, the four vendors.

## 1. your caterer

the catering team is usually the largest group of people working your wedding, and the one couples most often under-tip because they only think of the captain.

the standard: **$50 to $100 per server.** tip the team, not just the captain.

that means if you have six servers plus two bartenders, you're budgeting for eight people, not one. at $75 each, that's $600. it feels like a lot in a lump, but per person it's right in line with what these folks expect for a long day on their feet.

### watch the "service charge" trap

before you assume you owe tips on top of everything, check the contract for a service charge. a lot of catering invoices tack on an 18 to 22 percent service charge. sometimes that's distributed to staff as gratuity. sometimes it's a facility fee and the staff see none of it. the only way to know is to ask directly: "does the service charge go to the team as a tip, or is it a house fee?" get the answer in writing. if it's a house fee, budget the per-server cash on top. if it already covers gratuity, you can tip a smaller amount for standout service and keep the rest of your money.

## 2. your photographer

your photographer (and their second shooter) are with you longer than almost anyone else on the day. they show up before hair and makeup and they're still shooting during the last dance.

the standard: **$100 to $200 each.** this covers both your primary shooter and your second shooter.

so if you booked a photographer with a second shooter, you're looking at two envelopes, roughly $200 to $400 total. couples routinely forget the second shooter exists until they show up, which is exactly how one of the two people who captured your entire wedding goes home un-thanked.

### one exception worth knowing

if your photographer is the owner of the business, tipping is genuinely optional, because they set their own rates and keep the profit. it's still a warm gesture, and plenty of couples do it, but it's not the same unwritten expectation as tipping an employee or a contracted second shooter. a heartfelt review and a few referrals can mean as much to an owner as cash. when in doubt, tip the second shooter, who is almost always hired help, and use your judgment on the owner.

## 3. your dj

the dj controls the energy of your entire reception, and their tip flexes based on how the night actually goes.

the standard: **$50 to $150 flat,** depending on how late the night ran. more if they stayed late or set up early.

think of the range as a sliding scale:

1. **standard four-hour reception, everything on schedule:** the low end, around $50 to $75.
2. **they read the room, took requests, kept the floor packed:** the middle, around $100.
3. **they showed up two hours early for setup, or your reception ran past the contracted end time and they stayed:** the top of the range, $150 or a little more.

the "set up early" part matters. dj setup is unpaid labor that happens before your guests arrive, and it's invisible to you because you're busy getting married. if your dj rolled in at 2pm to run cable for a 6pm reception, that effort deserves the higher end.

## 4. your florist

this is the one almost nobody budgets for, and it's often the biggest tip on the list because it's a percentage instead of a flat rate.

the standard: **10 to 20 percent of the floral total.**

here's why it stings if you skip it: florals are expensive, so a percentage-based tip scales up fast. run the numbers.

- if your floral total is **$3,000**, a 15 percent tip is **$450.**
- at 10 percent, that same order is **$300.**
- at 20 percent, it's **$600.**

that is not envelope-of-loose-cash money. that is a plan-ahead-for-it number, and it's exactly the tip that catches couples off guard because it never appears in the floral proposal.

### how to handle a big floral tip

you don't have to tip the full 10 to 20 percent to every florist in every situation. the percentage assumes a florist who's doing full-service design: an elaborate install, on-site setup, and breakdown at the end of the night. if you ordered simple bouquets and centerpieces for pickup with no delivery or setup, a smaller flat tip is reasonable. the tip is really compensating labor and coordination, so scale it to how much of that labor your florist is actually doing on the day.

## red flags to watch for in your contracts

before you decide who gets what, read the fine print. a few things to catch:

- **a "service charge" that isn't gratuity.** as covered above, this is the number one reason couples accidentally over-tip or under-tip their caterer. always confirm in writing where it goes.
- **"gratuity included" language buried in a package.** some photographers, djs, and planners fold a tip into their all-in package price. if it's already in there, you don't owe it again. read for it.
- **mandatory gratuity on the bar.** many bar packages add an automatic 20 percent. that covers your bartenders. don't tip them separately unless someone went above and beyond.
- **delivery and setup fees mistaken for tips.** a florist's delivery fee pays for the truck and the gas, not the person's labor. it is not a substitute for a tip.
- **vague "staff appreciation" fees.** if you see a fee you don't understand, ask exactly who receives it. vendors expect this question from organized couples.
