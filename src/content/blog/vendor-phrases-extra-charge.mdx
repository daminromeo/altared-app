You sit down with a florist quote you love. The proposal is beautiful. The number at the top feels doable. Three months later the final invoice arrives and it's $600 higher, and when you ask why, someone points to a line you skimmed: "priced at market." Nobody lied to you. The word "market" was right there. It just read fine the first time and cost you the second time.

That's the thing about vendor contract language. The language in your contracts is doing a lot of quiet work. None of it is hidden. It's written in a way that sounds reassuring on a first pass and adds to your total on the second one. Below are seven phrases that quietly mean "extra charge," what each one actually does to your number, and how to catch it before you sign.

## The phrases that read fine and cost you later

Here's the fast version. Save this list, then read the breakdowns.

1. **Market price** — no cap on the number. Florists use this most.
2. **Administrative fee** — 5 to 10 percent tacked on for emails and paperwork.
3. **Travel included** — included up to X miles, then you pay per mile.
4. **Starting at** — the real number often lands $800 to $2,000 above the starting figure.
5. **Gratuity suggested** — sits on top of a 20 to 22 percent service charge already baked in.
6. **Comp'd setup** — breakdown and strike are still billed, often $150 to $400.
7. **Flexible pricing** — almost always flexes in one direction: up.

### 1. "Market price"

"Market price" means there's no ceiling on the number. It shows up most often with florists, because flower costs genuinely move with the season, supply, and what's available the week of your wedding. That part is honest. The problem is that "priced at market" transfers all of that risk onto you. If peonies spike, your bill spikes, and you agreed to it in writing.

You don't have to reject the phrase outright. You just need a boundary around it. Ask your florist for a "not to exceed" number, a cap you both sign off on. Or ask them to build the design around in-season blooms so the market can't move much. A good florist will happily give you a ceiling. If they won't put any number on it, that's information too.

### 2. "Administrative fee"

An administrative fee is 5 to 10 percent tacked on for emails, contracts, and general paperwork. Sometimes it's labeled "processing" or "coordination fee" instead. On a $6,000 catering contract, a 10 percent admin fee is $600 for work that has nothing to do with the food on your plate.

Admin fees aren't automatically a scam. Some vendors use them to cover real back-office labor. But you deserve to know what it covers and whether it's negotiable. Ask directly: "What does the administrative fee include, and is it a percentage or a flat amount?" A percentage fee grows every time you add to your order, so it's worth knowing before you upgrade the bar package or add 20 guests.

### 3. "Travel included"

"Travel included" almost always means included up to a mileage limit you may not have hit yet. Past that line, you pay per mile, and sometimes lodging on top. This one catches couples with venues outside the city, destination weekends, or a photographer who's "local" to a metro area that's technically an hour from your barn.

Read for the actual number. The contract will usually say something like "travel included within 30 miles of studio." Measure the real distance from the vendor to your venue, not to your apartment. If you're over the limit, ask for the per-mile rate and whether an overnight stay gets billed, then add that line into your budget now instead of discovering it in month eight.

## Why "starting at" is the most expensive phrase on the list

### 4. "Starting at"

"Starting at" is rarely where the final invoice lands. The real number is often $800 to $2,000 above the starting figure. "Packages starting at $2,500" means $2,500 buys the smallest package that technically exists, the one nobody actually books, usually with the fewest hours and no add-ons.

The fix is simple and slightly uncomfortable: ask what a couple like you actually spends. "For a Saturday in June, 120 guests, eight hours of coverage, what did your last three couples land at?" Vendors know this number. Getting it moves you from the marketing figure to the realistic one, which is the only figure your budget cares about. ### 5. "Gratuity suggested"

Here's a stacking trap. "Gratuity suggested" often sits on top of a service charge already baked into the contract, and that service charge is usually 20 to 22 percent. So the vendor collects a 20 to 22 percent service charge, then a suggested gratuity line invites you to add more, and many couples assume the service charge was the tip. It usually isn't. Service charges frequently go to the business, not the staff.

Before you tip, ask one question: "Does the service charge go to the staff, or is a separate gratuity expected?" On a $10,000 catering bill, a 22 percent service charge is $2,200. If you then add a 15 to 20 percent "suggested" gratuity, that's another $1,500 to $2,000 you may have budgeted zero dollars for. You might still choose to tip generously, and that's great. Just make it a decision, not a surprise.

### 6. "Comp'd setup"

"Comp'd setup" sounds like a gift. It means the vendor won't charge you to set up. What it quietly leaves out is the other end of the night. Breakdown and strike, the teardown and haul-out after your last dance, are still billed, and that line often runs $150 to $400.

When you see "complimentary setup" or "comp'd setup," ask about the back end: "Is breakdown and strike included, or billed separately?" This shows up with rentals, lighting, floral installs, and decor. The setup being free tells you nothing about the teardown, and the teardown is real labor at the end of a long night.

### 7. "Flexible pricing"

"Flexible pricing" almost always means the quote flexes in one direction. The number adjusts upward as your date gets closer, as you add guests, as you make changes, and as availability tightens. "Flexible" sounds like it bends toward you. In practice it bends toward the vendor.

If a quote is described as flexible, pin it down. Ask what specifically causes the price to change, and get a locked rate in writing wherever you can. A date, a guest count, an hourly rate, a package total: the more you can freeze, the less "flexible" works against you.

## Red flags to watch for before you sign

None of these phrases automatically means a vendor is dishonest. Plenty of great vendors use standard industry language. The red flags are about how they respond when you ask, not the words themselves.

- **They won't put a cap on "market price."** A florist who refuses any not-to-exceed number is asking you to sign a blank check.
- **The admin or service charge is a percentage with no explanation.** Percentages grow with your order. If nobody can tell you what the fee covers, treat it as a real cost, not a footnote.
- **"Starting at" and no willingness to share real averages.** If a vendor dodges the "what do couples actually spend" question, assume the gap between the starting figure and your invoice is wide.
- **Gratuity and service charge both appear with no clarity on where the money goes.** Ask once. Vagueness here can cost you thousands.
- **Vague teardown language.** "Setup included" with silence on breakdown is a $150 to $400 line waiting to appear.
- **"Flexible pricing" with nothing locked in writing.** Flexibility that only benefits the vendor isn't flexibility, it's a moving target.

The pattern across all seven: the fee is disclosed, but the framing makes it easy to skim. Your defense is reading the second time as carefully as the vendor wrote it the first time, and asking plain questions before your signature is on the page.

## What to do before you sign

Take this with you the next time you sit down with a contract.

1. **Search the quote for all seven phrases**, plus their cousins: "processing," "coordination fee," "complimentary," "package starting."
2. **Ask for a cap on anything "market" or "flexible."** Get a not-to-exceed number in writing.
3. **Confirm what every fee covers** and whether it's a percentage or flat, since percentages grow with your order.
4. **Measure real venue distance** against the travel/mileage limit, and price the overage now.
5. **Ask what couples like you actually spend**, not the "starting at" figure.
6. **Get clarity on service charge versus gratuity**, and where the money goes, before you tip.
7. **Confirm breakdown and strike** are either included or budgeted as a separate $150 to $400 line.
