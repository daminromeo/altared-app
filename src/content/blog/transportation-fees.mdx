You found a limo company with great reviews, called for a quote, and the hourly rate sounded totally doable. You mentally slotted it into your budget, felt good about it, and moved on to the next vendor. Then the contract arrived, and the number at the bottom was a few hundred dollars higher than the one you'd been quoting your fiancé all week.

Here's the thing worth knowing before you sign anything: none of that extra money is a scam. The limo quote looks reasonable until you add the fuel surcharge, the hour minimum they don't mention upfront, the gratuity already baked into the contract, and the deadhead miles they charge just to drive out to your venue. These are industry standard. But standard doesn't mean you have to be surprised by them.

Knowing all four before you sign means the final invoice matches what you expected. Let's walk through each one so the next transportation quote that lands in your inbox holds no surprises.

## The four fees hiding behind a "reasonable" hourly rate

Transportation is one of those wedding line items that seems simple. You need to get from point A to point B in something nicer than your Honda. But the way transportation companies price their services means the sticker number and the final number are almost never the same. Four things account for most of that gap.

### 1. The fuel surcharge

The fuel surcharge gets tacked on at 10 to 15 percent, and it's calculated after the hourly rate. That "after" matters. It's not folded into the number they gave you over the phone. It's a percentage applied on top.

So if your hourly rate math comes out to $600 for the vehicle, a 15 percent fuel surcharge adds $90 you weren't planning on. On a bigger package, that percentage stacks up quickly. A $1,200 base becomes $1,380 before you've added anything else.

Fuel surcharges exist because gas prices move and companies don't want to re-quote every time the market shifts. Fair enough. But it means the honest question to ask isn't "what's your hourly rate," it's "what's the fuel surcharge and is it calculated on the base or the total." Get that number in writing.

### 2. The minimum hours

Most limos require a 3 to 4 hour minimum, even for a 45-minute ride. This one catches people who only need transportation for a short, specific window, like getting the couple from the ceremony to the reception across town.

You might genuinely only need the car for 45 minutes. It doesn't matter. If the minimum is four hours, you pay for four hours. The company blocks that vehicle and driver off for your event, and they're not going to squeeze in another booking around your 45-minute gap.

The workaround isn't to fight the minimum, it's to use the hours you're already paying for. If you're committed to four hours regardless, plan the day so the car actually earns its keep. Think:

1. Pick up the couple and the wedding party from the getting-ready location
2. Handle the ceremony-to-reception transfer
3. Run a shuttle loop for a few key guests who don't have rides
4. Stage a getaway exit at the end of the night

You paid for the block either way. Stretching it across the day turns a "wasted" minimum into genuine value.

### 3. The gratuity

Gratuity is often 15 to 20 percent, pre-baked into the contract, and not optional. This is the one that surprises couples the most, because most of us think of a tip as something we decide on at the end based on service.

With transportation, the gratuity is frequently written directly into the contract as a mandatory line item. That's not the company being sneaky, it's how driver compensation works in the industry. But it does mean you should not budget an additional cash tip on top unless you specifically want to reward exceptional service, and you should absolutely not assume the price you were quoted includes it.

On a $1,200 base, an 18 percent built-in gratuity is another $216. Add that to the fuel surcharge and you're already several hundred dollars past the "reasonable" quote, and we haven't even talked about mileage yet.

### 4. The deadhead miles

Deadhead miles might be the least intuitive fee on this list. You pay for the drive to reach you, both ways. The vehicle doesn't teleport to your venue. It has to leave the company's garage, drive out to you, and then, at the end of the night, drive back. Deadhead miles are the charge for that empty travel time when no passengers are in the car.

If your venue is out in wine country, on a coast, or anywhere an hour-plus from the nearest transportation company, deadhead miles can be one of the largest add-ons in the whole quote. A remote venue that felt like a steal on the venue contract can quietly cost you a fortune in transportation because every vendor has to drive out to reach it.

This is why the same limo package can cost wildly different amounts for two couples. It's not the vehicle. It's the distance the vehicle travels empty to get to you and back.

## Watch for these red flags in a transportation quote

Once you know the four fees exist, the warning signs in a quote get easy to spot. Keep an eye out for:

- **A quote that only lists an hourly rate.** If the fuel surcharge, gratuity, and mileage aren't itemized, they're not gone, they're just not shown yet. Ask for the all-in number before you get attached.
- **No mention of the minimum.** If nobody tells you the minimum is 3 to 4 hours, ask directly. Assuming you can book "just the one ride" is how the invoice balloons.
- **Vague gratuity language.** "Gratuity may apply" is not an answer. You want the exact percentage and confirmation of whether it's already in the total.
- **Silence on travel to your venue.** If your venue is remote and the quote doesn't mention deadhead or travel fees, that number is coming later. Bring it up first.
- **Pressure to sign before you've seen an itemized breakdown.** Any reputable company will show you the line items. If they won't, that's your sign to slow down.

None of these mean the vendor is dishonest. They usually just mean the first quote was a starting point, not a final price. Your job is to turn the starting point into the real number before you commit.

## How to compare quotes so the final invoice matches

The trap with transportation is comparing two quotes that aren't actually comparable. One company shows you an all-in price with everything itemized. Another shows you a bare hourly rate. On paper, the second looks cheaper. In reality, once you add the fuel surcharge at 10 to 15 percent, the built-in gratuity at 15 to 20 percent, the 3 to 4 hour minimum, and deadhead miles, the "cheaper" quote might be the more expensive one.

Before you sign a single contract, do this:

1. Get every quote broken down into line items, not just a total
2. Confirm the fuel surcharge percentage and whether it applies to the base or the full amount
3. Ask the minimum number of hours in writing
4. Confirm whether gratuity is already included and at what percentage
5. Ask specifically about deadhead or travel charges for your venue's location
6. Add all four on top of the base yourself and compare the real bottom-line numbers

Only when both quotes include the same four fees are you comparing apples to apples. ## The quick version to save

Transportation isn't where weddings usually go wrong, but it's a classic spot for the final number to drift a few hundred dollars past the quote. Here's what to keep in your back pocket before you book a single car:

- **Fuel surcharge:** 10 to 15 percent, added after the hourly rate, not baked into the number you were quoted
- **Minimum hours:** most limos require 3 to 4 hours even for a 45-minute ride, so plan the day to use the block
- **Gratuity:** often 15 to 20 percent, pre-baked into the contract and not optional, so don't double-tip by accident
- **Deadhead miles:** you pay for the drive to reach you both ways, which hits hardest for remote venues
- **Always get an itemized breakdown** and add all four on top of the base before you compare quotes or sign

Knowing these four before you sign is the whole game. The fees aren't hidden to trip you up, they're just standard. Standard is a lot easier to plan for when you saw it coming.
