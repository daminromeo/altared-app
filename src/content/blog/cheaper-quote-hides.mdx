you got three quotes back and one was $800 less than the others. it felt like a win. you screenshotted it, sent it to your mom, maybe even told your partner you'd figured this part out. one vendor down, and you saved money doing it.

then the final invoice landed. setup fee. travel surcharge. an overtime clause you skimmed past. a service charge sitting on top of gratuity. by the time you added it all up, the "cheapest" vendor was the most expensive one in the room.

this happens constantly, and it's not because vendors are scamming you. it's because quotes are built to look clean, and the line items that push the number up live three pages deep in the contract. you won't catch them on a first read. and almost nobody compares them side by side, which is exactly when they'd show up.

## the low quote is a feeling, not a number

when you see three numbers next to each other, your brain does something predictable: it grabs the smallest one and calls it the answer. it feels decisive. it feels like a win you can report back to the people asking how planning is going.

but that smallest number is the floor, not the total. it's the price of the vendor showing up and doing the most basic version of the job. everything that makes the day actually work (getting their gear into your venue, staying late enough to catch the last dance, the staff who plate the food) tends to live outside that headline figure.

so the quote isn't lying to you. it's just answering a narrower question than the one you're actually asking. you're asking "what will this cost me." the quote is answering "what does this start at." those are different questions, and the gap between them is where budgets quietly break.

the fix isn't distrust. it's translation. you have to turn every quote into the same unit (total dollars out of your account) before you can compare anything at all.

## plus fees: where the number really lives

"plus fees" is the phrase to burn into your memory. it's the tell. anytime a quote presents a base price and then references fees "as applicable" or "per contract," the real total is somewhere else in the document.

here's a concrete version of how it plays out. say a caterer quotes you $2,800. clean number, easy to say out loud, cheaper than the other two you got. then the fine print does its work:

1. **setup and breakdown fee.** they arrive early to stage the space and stay after to clear it. that labor is real, and it's often billed separately.
2. **travel surcharge.** your venue is outside their standard radius, so mileage and crew transport get added on.
3. **overtime clause.** your reception runs past the contracted end time, and each extra hour bills at a premium rate.
4. **service charge.** a percentage (frequently 18 to 22 percent) applied to the food and beverage subtotal, which people constantly confuse with the tip.
5. **gratuity.** and here's the trap: the service charge is usually not the tip. so if you tip on top, you're layering another chunk on the total.

stack those on the $2,800 base and that quote becomes $4,100 at final invoice. that's a $1,300 gap between the number you screenshotted and the number you paid. and if the "expensive" vendor across the street quoted $3,600 all-in with fewer add-ons, the cheap one just cost you more and you never saw it coming.

### the service charge vs. gratuity mixup

this one deserves its own beat because it fools people who otherwise read carefully. a service charge is a fee the business keeps to cover operations, staffing, and overhead. it does not necessarily reach the people serving you. gratuity is the tip. some contracts include gratuity in the service charge, some don't, and some are deliberately vague so you can't tell.

before you sign anything, ask one direct question: "does the service charge include gratuity, or is a tip expected on top?" get the answer in writing. that single sentence can swing your total by hundreds of dollars.

## why you never catch it on the first read

the fees aren't printed in bold on the cover page. they're not on slide one of the pitch. they're in section 7, paragraph 4, in the same font as everything else, phrased in language designed to sound routine.

on a first read you're skimming for the big stuff: date, headcount, deposit, what's included. your eyes glide past "a 20% service charge will be applied to all food and beverage" because it reads like boilerplate. it isn't. it's a number.

and here's the structural problem. you almost never read these contracts side by side. you get quote A on monday, quote B on thursday, quote C the following week. by the time C arrives, you're comparing it to your memory of A, not the actual document. your memory kept the headline price and dropped the fine print. so the comparison you *think* you're making (apples to apples) is actually the cover page of one versus the cover page of another, with all the fee structures invisible.

that's the real issue. not that any one quote is dishonest, but that the format makes honest comparison almost impossible without a spreadsheet and a couple of free evenings.

## red flags to watch for in a quote

when you're reading a quote, these are the phrases and patterns that mean the headline number is not the real number. treat each one as a question you need answered before you sign.

- **"starting at" or "from $X."** a range disguised as a price. ask what pushes it up and by how much.
- **"plus applicable fees" with no itemized list.** applicable to what? get every fee named with a dollar amount or a percentage.
- **a service charge with no mention of gratuity.** find out if the tip is included or expected separately.
- **vague overtime language.** "additional hours billed at prevailing rate" tells you nothing. get the per-hour number.
- **travel or delivery "to be determined."** to be determined by whom, and when? nail it down before the deposit, not after.
- **a total that's suspiciously round.** $2,800 flat usually means the messy real-world costs got moved into fees elsewhere.
- **pressure to sign fast to "lock in the price."** the price you're locking in might be the floor, not the ceiling.

none of these automatically mean a vendor is bad. a great photographer or caterer might have every one of these and still be worth it. the point is to know the whole number before you commit, so you're choosing with real information instead of a feeling.

## how to actually compare quotes

the goal is simple: get every quote into the same format so you're comparing what you'll actually pay, not what the cover page says. here's the process.

1. **list every line item from each quote in one column.** base price, then each fee, spelled out.
2. **convert every percentage into a dollar amount.** a "20% service charge" is meaningless until you multiply it against the actual food and beverage total. do the math.
3. **add a row for the things not in the quote at all.** overtime you expect to use, gratuity if it's on top of the service charge, taxes.
4. **total each vendor to a single all-in number.** that's the only number that matters.
5. **then rank them.** now the $2,800 quote that becomes $4,100 sits honestly next to the $3,600 quote that stays $3,600, and the "expensive" one might win.

## the takeaway

the low quote is a feeling. the final invoice is the number. your whole job is to close the distance between them before you sign, not after.
