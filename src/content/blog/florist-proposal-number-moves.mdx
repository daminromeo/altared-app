A bride in March books her florist for an October wedding. The proposal is clean: itemized arrangements, a labor line, a delivery fee, a total at the bottom that lands right where she hoped. She signs. In September she gets an updated invoice. The total is $620 higher. Nothing was added. The garden roses she picked were priced at "market" in the spring, and market in October was not market in March.

She didn't miss a hidden fee. She missed a line that was never fixed in the first place. It read exactly like every other line.

This is the thing nobody warns you about with florist proposals. you read the total, the total feels fine, and you assume the total is a promise. but somewhere inside that quote, in the stem count or the labor estimate or the small "market price" caveat, there's a number that isn't locked. and it won't announce itself.

## why florist proposals feel complete when they aren't

Most florist proposals are written to feel complete. They're not. A florist isn't trying to trick you. They're pricing flowers, and flowers are an agricultural product with a supply chain, a season, and a wholesale market that moves week to week. So they protect themselves with language. The problem is that the protective language sits right next to the firm language, in the same font, in the same column, with a dollar amount beside it that looks just as settled as the rest.

You see "$1,200 — bridal party florals" and you read a quote. But if the fine print says "based on seasonal availability" or "subject to minimum arrangement sizing," that $1,200 is an estimate wearing a quote's clothes. The hard part is that it reads like every other line.

A single caveat about seasonal availability or minimum sizing can shift your total by $400 or $800 before the delivery truck arrives. On a $5,000 flower budget, that's the difference between on-track and over. And you usually find out late, when the deposit is gone and rebooking isn't realistic.

### the difference between a quote and an estimate

This distinction is worth sitting with, because florists rarely spell it out.

A **quote** is a fixed price. The florist is committing to deliver a specific thing for a specific number. If costs rise, that's their risk, not yours.

An **estimate** is a best guess. It moves with conditions. If peonies double in price that month, the estimate moves with them, and the new number lands on your invoice.

Most proposals are a blend of both, and they don't label which lines are which. Your job is to find the estimates hiding inside the quote.

## the lines that move (and the ones that don't)

Here are the lines most likely to shift between signing and delivery, roughly in order of how often they cause a surprise:

1. **"Market price" or "current market" stems.** This is the loudest quiet line. Premium flowers (peonies, garden roses, ranunculus, certain orchids) are often priced at market because their wholesale cost genuinely swings. If your proposal lists a count of these without a locked per-stem price, that's a moving number.
2. **Seasonal availability caveats.** A line that says "subject to seasonal availability" means your florist may substitute, and substitutions can cost more. Out-of-season peonies in October aren't cheaper than in-season ones in June. They're flown in.
3. **Minimum arrangement sizing.** Some quotes assume a minimum size per centerpiece or installation. If the florist later decides the look "needs more" to read right in your venue, the sizing floor rises and so does the count.
4. **Labor estimates.** Setup, breakdown, and installation hours are frequently estimated, not fixed. A big arch or hanging installation that takes longer than planned can add labor you never saw quoted.
5. **Delivery and travel.** Usually firmer, but watch for "estimated mileage" or fees that depend on final timeline (a second trip for breakdown late at night, for example).
6. **Rental returns.** Vases, candleholders, and stands sometimes carry a deposit or a replacement charge that only shows up if an item goes missing after the event.

Items 1 through 3 are where the $400 to $800 swings usually live. Items 4 through 6 tend to be smaller, but they add up.

### a real-money example

Say your proposal reads like this:

- Bridal bouquet (garden roses, market price): $250
- 12 centerpieces at $120 each: $1,440
- Ceremony arch (seasonal florals, sizing as needed): $900
- Labor (estimated 6 hours): $480
- Delivery: $150
- **Total: $3,220**

Looks fixed. Feels fixed. But three of those lines are soft. If garden roses run high that season, the bouquet creeps to $310. If the arch "needs more" to fill the space and sizing climbs, that $900 becomes $1,200. If labor runs eight hours instead of six, add $160. You've now paid roughly $3,650 for a proposal that said $3,220, and not one line was technically wrong. That's a $430 swing inside the $400 to $800 range, on a fairly modest order.

## red flags to watch for in a florist proposal

You don't need to be a flower expert to catch most of these. You need to read for language, not just numbers. Watch for:

- **"Market price," "current pricing," or "subject to availability."** Any of these next to a dollar amount means that amount can move. Ask for it to be capped or locked.
- **"Sizing as needed" or "arrangements may vary."** Translation: the count or volume isn't final, and bigger costs more.
- **"Estimated" anywhere near labor or hours.** Ask what happens if the actual hours exceed the estimate, and whether there's a ceiling.
- **A total with no itemization.** If you can't see the stem counts and per-line pricing, you can't see what's soft. A lump sum is the easiest place to hide a moving number.
- **No substitution policy.** If the proposal mentions seasonal flowers but says nothing about what happens when they're unavailable, get that in writing. "Substitutions of equal or lesser value at no additional cost" is the sentence you want.
- **Verbal reassurance that isn't on paper.** "Don't worry, that price is basically locked" is not a locked price. If it's locked, it should say so in the document.

The pattern across all of these: the risk reads like every other line. It's calm, professional, and easy to skim past. That's exactly why it works.

## what to do before you sign

The fix isn't to distrust your florist. Good florists are upfront, and the variability is real. The fix is to know which numbers are soft so you can plan, negotiate, or pad your budget on purpose instead of by accident.

Before you sign, do this:

1. **Ask which lines are quotes and which are estimates.** Say it plainly: "Which of these prices can change between now and my date?" A good florist will tell you in thirty seconds.
2. **Ask for caps on the soft lines.** "Can we cap the market-price stems so I know my ceiling?" Many florists will agree to a not-to-exceed number.
3. **Get the substitution policy in writing.** Equal or lesser value, no upcharge. This single sentence protects you from the out-of-season surprise.
4. **Confirm the labor ceiling.** Ask what triggers extra hours and whether you'll be told before the clock keeps running.
5. **Build a buffer for whatever stays soft.** If two lines genuinely can't be locked, set aside roughly 10 to 15 percent of those lines so the swing doesn't break your total.

## the short version

The total at the bottom of your florist proposal is only as fixed as its softest line. Before you sign:

- Read for language, not just numbers. "Market price," "seasonal," and "estimated" are the words that move money.
- Ask your florist directly which lines can change, and get the answer in writing.
- Cap the soft lines or get a not-to-exceed number where you can.
- Lock the substitution policy at "equal or lesser value, no upcharge."
- Pad a 10 to 15 percent buffer on anything that stays variable.

A $400 to $800 swing isn't a disaster when you saw it coming. It's a disaster when you find out in September. Find the number that moves while you still have room to do something about it.
