You sat through the tasting. You picked the almond with raspberry, said no to the fondant, chose three tiers with a clean buttercream finish. The baker wrote up a proposal, you saw a number you could live with, and you signed. Then, weeks later, the venue invoice shows up and there is a line for a "cake cutting fee" you never agreed to, a delivery charge you assumed was baked in (it wasn't), and a florist add-on for the flowers sitting on top of tier two.

None of those numbers were on the baker's quote. All of them are real. The cake quote is not the cake total, and the gap between the two is where couples get surprised at final billing.

Here are the three costs that almost never show up in the proposal but always show up on the invoice, plus exactly how to catch them before you sign.

## 01. The cutting fee comes from your venue, not your baker

This is the one that catches the most people off guard, because it's charged by a completely different vendor than the one who made the cake.

A cutting fee runs $2 to $5 per slice, and it's billed by your venue, not your baker. Some venues frame it as a "cake service fee" or "plating fee." The logic is that their staff has to slice, plate, and serve every guest, and they charge for the labor and the china. Whether you think that's fair is a separate conversation. What matters is that it's standard, and it scales directly with your guest count.

Run the math and it stops feeling small:

1. 100 guests at $3 per slice is $300.
2. 150 guests at $4 per slice is $600.
3. 200 guests at $5 per slice is $1,000.

That last number is not a rounding error. For a 200-person wedding, the cutting fee alone can cost more than a modest cake. And because it lives on the venue contract instead of the baker proposal, couples who only scrutinize the bakery quote never see it coming.

### how to handle it

Ask your venue point-blank: "do you charge a cake cutting or plating fee, and what is the per-slice rate?" Get the answer in writing before you sign the venue contract, because that's the document where this hides. If the fee feels steep, some couples negotiate it down or waive it by ordering dessert through the venue's in-house catering. Others skip a formal cut-and-serve entirely and do a dessert table or a self-serve setup, which sidesteps the per-plate labor. There's no single right answer, but there is a wrong one, and that's finding out on the final invoice.

## 02. Delivery and setup is almost always a separate line

A three-tier cake does not teleport to your reception table. Someone has to box it, drive it (often in a temperature-controlled vehicle), carry it in, stack the tiers on site, and get the whole thing level and stable. That labor has a price, and delivery plus setup runs $150 to $300. It is almost never included in the base cake quote.

The reason this one surprises people is wording. A proposal might list "3-tier buttercream cake, serves 120: $650" and stop there. It reads like a complete number. But "the cake" and "getting the cake safely to your venue and assembled" are two different services, and plenty of bakers itemize them separately (or don't list delivery at all until you ask).

### why you almost never want to DIY this

Every year, couples try to save the $150 to $300 by picking the cake up themselves. Tiered cakes are heavy, fragile, and sensitive to heat and every bump in the road. Assembling multiple tiers on site is genuinely a skill. If your uncle's SUV takes a hard turn and the top tier slides, you've lost the whole thing on the morning of your wedding, and there's no do-over. For a single-tier cake, DIY pickup can be reasonable. For anything stacked or delicate, the delivery line is buying you insurance, not just a car ride.

### questions to ask the baker

- Is delivery and setup included in this quote, or is it a separate line?
- What's the fee, and does it change based on how far my venue is?
- Do you assemble the tiers on site, or do I receive it pre-stacked?
- Is there an extra charge if setup runs long or requires waiting?

## 03. Fresh florals get placed by your florist, and that costs extra

Those loose garden roses cascading down the side of the cake in your inspo photo did not grow there. Fresh flowers on a cake almost always come from your florist, not your baker, and your florist charges separately to place them. Budget $75 to $200 extra for that placement.

There are actually two hidden pieces bundled inside this one line:

First, the flowers themselves. Cake florals are usually pulled from your larger floral order or arranged as a small dedicated piece, and either way they add to the florist's total.

Second, the placement labor. Fresh blooms have to be prepped so they're food-safe (stems wrapped, no direct contact with parts of the cake that touch food) and arranged by hand, on site, after the cake is set up. That coordination between your baker and your florist is real work, and it shows up as a line on the florist invoice, not the cake quote.

If you assumed the baker would just "add some flowers," you're picturing a service that two different vendors quietly split between them. Neither one may mention the other's fee, which is exactly how it slips through.

### a cheaper path if you want the look

Sugar flowers made by the baker can cost more than fresh ones because they're labor-intensive, so that's not automatically the budget move. But if the fresh-floral placement fee stings, ask your florist for a simple loose scatter of a few blooms rather than an engineered cascade, or use greenery, which is cheaper than premium flowers. You get the styled look at a fraction of the placement cost.

## Watch for these red flags in your cake quote

A clean-looking proposal is not the same as a complete one. Here's what should make you pause and ask a follow-up question:

- **A single all-in number with no itemization.** "$650, three tiers" tells you nothing about delivery, setup, or service. Ask for the breakdown.
- **The word "starting at."** A per-slice or per-serving price that starts at a number almost always climbs with flavor upgrades, fillings, and finish.
- **No mention of delivery anywhere.** Silence isn't "included." It usually means "ask us and we'll add it."
- **Flowers shown in the design but absent from the line items.** Someone is placing those, and someone is billing for it.
- **A tasting fee that isn't credited.** Some bakers charge for the tasting and apply it to your order, others just charge. Confirm which.
- **Vague cake-versus-venue overlap.** If nobody has told you who cuts and serves, the cutting fee is lurking on the venue side.

The pattern across all three costs is the same: the charge lives on a different document than the one you're reading. The cutting fee is on the venue contract. Delivery is often only added when you ask the baker directly. Florals land on the florist invoice. You can love every tier of that cake and still get surprised at final billing, purely because no single piece of paper shows you the full picture.

## Put it together before you sign

Before your tasting appointment, and definitely before you sign anything, run these five steps:

1. Ask your venue for the cutting or plating fee per slice, and multiply it by your guest count.
2. Confirm with your baker whether delivery and setup is included or a separate line, and get the dollar figure.
3. Ask your florist what they charge to place fresh blooms on the cake, and whether the flowers themselves are inside or outside your main order.
4. Add all three to the base cake price to get your true cake total, then compare that against your dessert budget.
5. Get every one of these answers in writing so the invoice matches the conversation.

Do that, and the cake stops being a budget landmine. You'll know your real number going in, which means you can decide what's worth it (delivery, almost always) and what you'd rather trim (a $1,000 cutting fee on a 200-guest list is very negotiable).
