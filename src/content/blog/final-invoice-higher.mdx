you got the quote. you loved the number. you signed.

and then the final invoice landed, and it was $4,000 more than you expected. the worst part? somehow it was all technically correct. nobody lied to you. nobody snuck anything in under cover of darkness. every single charge had a line, a label, and a place in the contract you signed weeks ago.

service charges at 22%. an admin fee you didn't notice on page 3. an overtime clause that kicked in at 9pm. none of it was hidden exactly. it was just written in a way that made it easy to miss until it was too late to do anything about it.

that gap between the quote and the invoice is one of the most predictable, most avoidable, and most emotionally brutal parts of wedding planning. so let's break down exactly where it comes from, what to look for before you sign, and how to know your real number before you're locked in.

## the quote is a starting point, not a price

here's the mental shift that saves people the most money: a quote is a sales document. it exists to get you excited and to get you to sign. the contract is the legal document. it exists to define what you actually owe. those two things are almost never the same number, and the difference can run up to 30% of the quote you fell in love with.

think about what a quote usually shows you. it shows the pretty stuff. the per-plate price. the base venue rate. the photographer's collection price. what it tends to leave off, or bury below the fold, is the machinery that turns that clean number into a real total.

that machinery usually includes:

1. **service charges** (often 18–22%, and often applied before tax, not after)
2. **admin or "administrative" fees**, sometimes a flat amount and sometmes a percentage
3. **gratuity**, which may or may not be included in the service charge (you have to read carefully)
4. **overtime clauses** with a specific cutoff time and a per-hour rate
5. **taxes** applied on top of the service charge, not just the base
6. **delivery, setup, and breakdown fees** for rentals, florals, and catering
7. **cake-cutting, corkage, or "outside vendor" fees**

any one of these on its own feels small. stacked together on a $20,000 catering contract, a 22% service charge alone is $4,400. that's the whole game. you didn't overspend. you just never saw the full number.

## the fine print, line by line

let's walk through the three culprits from that $4,000 surprise, because they show up again and again.

### service charges at 22%

this is the big one. a service charge is a percentage the vendor adds to your subtotal, and it's easy to assume it's the same as tip. it usually isn't. read the language. if the contract says "a 22% service charge will be applied," ask directly, in writing: does this go to staff as gratuity, or is it a house fee? if it's a house fee, you may still be expected to tip on top of it.

and watch the order of operations. some contracts apply the service charge to the subtotal, then apply sales tax to the new, higher number. so you're paying tax on the service charge. on a $20,000 food and beverage minimum, that stacking can add well over $5,000 before you've upgraded a single thing.

### the admin fee on page 3

the admin fee is the quiet one. it lives past the exciting part of the contract, usually as a flat line item with no explanation. it might be $500. it might be a separate 3–5% on top of the service charge. either way, it's rarely mentioned in the sales conversation, so it never makes it into your working budget. the fix is simple but you have to be deliberate: find every fee that isn't the base price, add them up, and treat that total as your real starting point.

### the overtime clause that kicks in at 9pm

overtime is the fee that turns a great night into a bad morning. your contract sets an end time, say 10pm, and defines what happens after: a per-hour rate for the venue, plus separately billed overtime for the band, the photographer, the coordinator, and the bar staff. one extra hour can quietly become four or five separate charges. couples routinely get hit with $800 to $2,000 in overtime because the party was good and nobody wanted to be the one to shut it down.

before you sign, know your hard stop, know the per-hour cost of pushing past it, and decide in advance whether you'd actually pay it. that decision is a thousand times cheaper to make sober at your kitchen table than emotional at 10:45pm on the dance floor.

## red flags to watch for before you sign

not every fee is a scam. service charges are standard. overtime rates are normal. the problem is vagueness and burial, not the existence of fees. here's what should make you slow down and ask questions:

- **"plus applicable fees and taxes" with no numbers attached.** applicable is doing a lot of work in that sentence. make them show you the actual percentages.
- **a service charge with no explanation of where it goes.** if you can't tell whether it's gratuity, ask in writing and get it in writing back.
- **fees that only appear in the contract, not the quote.** if page 3 has a charge that page 1 never mentioned, that's the gap you're looking for.
- **an overtime clause with no cutoff time named.** you can't plan around a rule you can't see.
- **"prices subject to change."** ask what's locked and what floats, especially for per-plate catering booked a year out.
- **vendors who get cagey when you ask for the all-in number.** a good vendor will happily walk you through the total. hesitation is information.

none of these means walk away. all of them mean ask, in writing, before you sign. the leverage you have as a couple is highest before your signature and near zero after. every question you ask up front is a number you won't be surprised by later.

## how to find your real number

the goal is to never be surprised by an invoice again. you do that by turning the quote into a true total before you commit, not after. here's the process:

1. **list the base price** from the quote, exactly as written.
2. **hunt down every percentage.** service charge, admin fee, and any facility or resort fee. write each one out as a dollar amount, not a percent, so you can feel the size of it.
3. **find every flat fee.** delivery, setup, breakdown, cake-cutting, corkage, coordinator, security.
4. **calculate tax correctly.** confirm whether tax applies to the base only or to the base plus service charge, then run the real math.
5. **read the overtime clause** and add one hour of overtime to your estimate as a buffer, so a fun night never blows the budget.
6. **add it all up.** that total, not the quote, is the number you compare across vendors and plan your budget around.

do this for every contract and you'll often find the true total lands 20% to 30% above the headline quote. that's not a reason to panic. it's a reason to plan. once you know the real number, you can negotiate, cut, or simply prepare for it instead of getting ambushed.
