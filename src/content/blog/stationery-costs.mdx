You get the invitation suite quote back and it looks reasonable. Maybe even better than you expected. You sign off, feel a little rush of progress, and move on to the next thing on the list. Then three months later the final invoice arrives and the number has quietly grown by hundreds of dollars. Nothing was hidden exactly. It was just never in the headline quote.

This is the most common way stationery budgets go sideways. Couples price the invites and forget the six things surrounding them. Envelope addressing, the day-of suite, postage, reprints, assembly, and venue signage all land later, and by the time they hit the invoice the total looks nothing like the original proposal.

Here's every line to check before you approve anything, with the dollar ranges to expect so nothing surprises you.

## the invitation suite is just the beginning

When a stationer sends a quote, the number you see usually covers the printed pieces of the main invitation: the invite card, an RSVP card, maybe a details card, plus the outer envelopes blank. That's it. It reads like the whole cost because it's the biggest single line and it's the thing you asked about.

But a finished, mailed, fully-signed wedding needs a lot more paper than that, and each additional piece is priced separately. The six costs below are the ones that almost always get added after you've already mentally filed stationery as "handled."

## 1. envelope addressing

Blank envelopes come with your suite. Addressed envelopes usually don't.

Hand calligraphy or even nice digital printing runs **$1.50 to $4 per envelope**, and it adds up faster than almost anything else because it scales directly with your guest count. If you have 120 households on your list, that's 120 outer envelopes, and if you're doing inner envelopes or return addressing too, you're multiplying again.

Do the quick math before you commit:

1. Count your mailing households (not total guests).
2. Multiply by the per-envelope rate your stationer quotes.
3. Add return addressing if you want it (often billed separately).
4. Add RSVP envelope addressing if you want those pre-filled too.

At 120 households and $3 per envelope, outer addressing alone is $360. That's a real line, and it rarely appears in the first proposal.

## 2. the day-of suite

Your invitations get people to the wedding. Your day-of suite runs the wedding once they arrive: programs, menus, escort cards, place cards, table numbers, seating charts, bar signs, favor tags. All the printed pieces guests actually touch during the event.

This is **often billed as a completely separate package**, sometimes by a different timeline and occasionally by a different designer. Couples who assume "stationery" was one number are surprised to learn the day-of suite is its own quote entirely.

If you want a cohesive look, and most couples do, price the day-of pieces at the same time you price invitations. Even if you order them later, knowing the number now keeps it from ambushing you in the final month when your budget has the least give. Our [budgeting](/blog/category/budgeting) posts have more on sequencing these payments so nothing clusters right before the wedding.

## 3. postage

Postage feels like it should be trivial. It is not, and the reason is weight and shape.

A thick invitation suite, one with multiple cards, a vellum wrap, a wax seal, or a bulky envelope, pushes past a single standard stamp. **Thick suites need $1.30+ stamps**, and non-standard shapes (square envelopes are the classic offender) get charged an extra surcharge on top of that.

The rule that saves you money: **weigh before you buy.** Take one fully assembled, sealed suite to the post office and have it weighed and measured before you buy postage for the whole batch. Buying stamps for the whole list based on a guess is how couples end up with a stack of assembled invitations that can't actually be mailed at the rate they planned for.

### don't forget the RSVP return postage

You're also expected to stamp the RSVP envelope so guests can mail it back. That's a second stamp per invite, and it's yours to pay. On a 120-household list that's 120 more stamps, easy to forget when you're focused on the outbound cost.

## 4. extra printing (reprints)

One misspelled name. A wrong date on the details card. A venue address with a transposed number. Any of these means a reprint, and reprints are not free.

**Typo reprints run $75 to $200**, depending on how much has to be redone and whether the press setup has to be repeated. If a mistake makes it past you and onto the printed pieces, you're paying to fix it even if the error was in the proof you approved.

That last part matters: once you sign off on a proof, the error becomes yours. So the defense here is simple and free. **Proofread every word**, out loud, more than once, and get a second set of eyes on it. Specifically check:

1. Every proper name (yours, your partner's, both families').
2. The date and the day of the week (make sure they actually match a calendar).
3. The start time.
4. The venue name and full address.
5. The website URL and RSVP deadline.

Reading it out loud catches what silent reading skips. A $200 reprint fee is a painful line to pay for a comma.

## 5. assembly fee

Somebody has to stuff each envelope, insert the cards in order, add any liner or ribbon or wax seal, and seal it. If that somebody is your stationer, it's a service, and it's billed.

**Stuffing and sealing is billed at $1 to $3 per suite.** At 120 households, that's another $120 to $360 on top of everything else, just to have the pieces put together and ready to mail.

You can absolutely do assembly yourself and save this line entirely. Just be honest about the time. Assembling 120 detailed suites with liners and seals is an evening or two of work, and if your suite has multiple inserts and a specific stacking order, it's slower than it sounds. The point isn't that assembly is a rip-off. The point is that it's a real cost you should decide on deliberately, not discover on the invoice.

## 6. venue signage

Welcome boards, seating chart displays, bar menus on foam or acrylic, table signs. Large-format printed signage is its own category, and it's priced by the piece.

**Welcome boards and table signs start at $80 each.** A single welcome sign is one thing. But once you add a seating chart, a couple of bar signs, and directional signage, you're looking at several hundred dollars in signage alone, and it's usually the last thing anyone budgets for because it feels like a day-of detail rather than a stationery cost.

## watch for these red flags in your quote

Before you approve anything, read the quote for what it doesn't say. These are the signs that costs are waiting offstage:

- **The quote lists "invitation suite" as one line with no itemization.** If addressing, assembly, and postage aren't broken out, assume they aren't included.
- **No mention of the day-of pieces at all.** Programs, menus, and escort cards are missing because they're a separate package, not because you don't need them.
- **Postage is your responsibility (it almost always is), but the suite's weight isn't listed.** You can't estimate stamps without it.
- **The proof approval language makes reprints your cost.** Standard, but know it's there before you sign.
- **Signage is quoted "per piece, TBD."** That "TBD" grows. Get a count and a rate now.
- **A round-number total with no per-envelope or per-suite math.** Numbers that don't break down are numbers that move.

If a stationer can't or won't itemize when you ask, that's your answer. A good one will walk you through exactly which of these six lines are in the quote and which aren't.

## run the real number before you sign

Here's what a mid-size list actually looks like once the surrounding costs are in. Say 120 households:

- Envelope addressing at $3 each: $360
- Assembly at $2 per suite: $240
- Outbound postage at $1.30 each: $156
- RSVP return postage: another ~$78
- A welcome board and two signs at $80+ each: $240+
- Day-of suite: its own package, often several hundred dollars

That's well over $1,000 sitting entirely outside the invitation quote you approved, and none of it was hidden. It just wasn't in the headline number.

## the actionable checklist

Save this for the moment you sit down with your stationer:

1. **Ask for an itemized quote**, not a single suite total.
2. **Count households**, then multiply for addressing and assembly yourself.
3. **Weigh one assembled suite** before buying any postage, and buy RSVP return stamps too.
4. **Price the day-of suite now**, even if you order it later.
5. **Proofread every word** and get a second reader before approving the proof.
6. **Get a signage count and per-piece rate** while you're at it.

The invitation is the part everyone sees. The other six lines are the part that decides whether your stationery budget holds. Price all of it up front and the final invoice won't surprise you.
